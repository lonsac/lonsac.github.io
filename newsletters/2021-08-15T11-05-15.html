Is There Anywhere With No Property Taxes?

What happened:

Tina Dupere lives with her mother in Dartmouth, Massachusetts in a home that has been in the family since 1976.

In 2010, Tina’s mother had a stroke, and Tina has been taking care of her ever since. In 2013, they started struggling to pay their property taxes.

The town sold the tax lien to a private company, Tallage Davis LLC, which added 16% interest. Tina managed to pay it off in 2016, but fell behind on taxes again later that year.

The same company bought the second tax lien when Tina and her mother owed a total of $12,000 in property taxes, excluding interest and fees, on the $330,000 home.

And because the family could not pay off this debt, Tallage Davis was allowed to foreclose on their home. It is now in the process of evicting them, and Massachusetts law will allow the company to keep all the equity in the home.

In other words, Tallage Davis will be able to take $300,000 from Tina Dupere and her mother, because they owed $12,000 in property taxes.

With the help of the Pacific Legal Foundation, Tina and her mother are suing for relief under the Fifth Amendment right against “uncompensated takings of private property,” the Eighth Amendment protection against “the imposition of excessive fines,” and the Fourteenth Amendment “right to adequate notice before being deprived of their property.”

Although the case is not closed, the Pacific Legal Foundation recently said that Tina and her mother will be able to stay in their home.

What this means:

Sadly, this is not a unique case. This sort of thing happens all over the country.

This is all the more sadly ironic given the CDC’s illegal eviction moratoriums that allows nonpaying tenants to remain on other people’s property, in the name of COVID-19.

But a family with health problems is being kicked out of their own property, because they owe money to the government.

In other words, your property isn’t really yours — you are the tenant of the government. And if you don’t pay your rent (property taxes) the government is not a forgiving landlord.

What you can do about it:

In the US, property taxes are assessed by municipalities, and therefore vary across towns and cities.

But in Sovereign Man’s in-depth guide on the lowest tax states, we highlight the states with the lowest average property taxes, among the states with no income taxes.

Here is the ranking of no income tax states from lowest average property tax, to highest:

  1. Nevada — .60%
  2. Wyoming — .61%
  3. Tennessee — .71%
  4. Florida — .89%
  5. Washington — .98%
  6. Alaska — 1.19%
  7. South Dakota — 1.31%
  8. Texas — 1.80%
  9. New Hampshire — 2.18%

In addition, Colorado, Utah, Indiana, and North Dakota each have a state income tax under 5%, and an average property tax under 1%.

There are some towns in the US which do not impose property taxes.

For example, Youngtown, Arizona has no property tax. Instead, it charges a 3% local sales tax, on top of the state’s 6.3% rate. It also has a 2% hotel tax and 2% rental tax.

The city of Stafford, Texas actually makes no property taxes a selling point. Unfortunately the county still charges what are effectively property taxes, even if it calls them by another name.

Your best bet to find a community in the US with no property taxes is to look in Alaska.

The Alaska department of commerce says that in Alaska, “for all its vastness only a small portion of the land mass is subject to a property tax.”

Only 24 of 165 municipalities in Alaska charge land and home owners property tax.

In addition, “Alaska exempts from property taxes the first $150,000 of assessed value for all senior citizens (65 years of age and over) and disabled veterans.”

Alaska also has no income tax, or sales tax. But municipalities can charge sales tax of up to 7%.

Notable Countries Without Property Taxes

Malta

According to the Commissioner for Revenue of Malta, “The legal system of Malta does not impose a tax on ownership of property. However, if you rent the property, tax is applicable on the rental income received.” And in most cases, an 8% tax on the total value of property transfers also applies.

Malta frequently factors into Plan B options as a jurisdiction friendly towards cryptocurrency, retirees, and digital nomads. Some of these programs are currently in flux, so keep an eye out for future updates if you are interested in Malta.

Cayman Islands

The Cayman Islands does not charge property tax, however it does charge a 7.5% (in most cases) “stamp duty” on the value of real estate at sale.

The Caymans is a very nice, safe, and developed place with no income tax. But residency through investment in the country costs at least $600,000 — much higher than what citizenship costs in other Caribbean countries. And citizenship is much more desirable than residency.

If owning your land outright and paying no property taxes is important to you, there are options available. As usual, you have to weigh the trade-offs — for example if cold, dark Alaskan winters are worth tax-free land.
