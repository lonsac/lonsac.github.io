Soil Fertility

At this link you can download a high quality version of the USDA National Resources Conservation Service global Inherent Land Quality Map.

Areas are colored based on soil performance — the ability to provide nutrients, regulate temperature, house micro-organisms etc.— and soil resilience — the ability to recover from damaging pollutants, over-farming, or natural disasters.

The map is shaded green for high performance and high resilience, dark blue for high performance and medium resilience, and light blue for high resilience and medium performance. These are the areas of the globe with the best inherent soil quality.

Some notable large, high quality areas include:

  • Much of the central United States, as well as California, and the southeastern coast
  • Much of Uruguay and northeastern Argentina
  • Areas north of the Black Sea including parts of Ukraine, Romania, Bulgaria and Russia.

But you can find pockets of quality soil in almost any country you may consider living in. Just reference the map when searching for property. And when you get far enough into the process of actually visiting land, bring along a cheap soil testing kit to confirm what you’re working with.

Water Availability

You probably don’t want to rely on rivers for irrigation. You’d be at the mercy of upstream landowners, more subject to environmental laws, and have to compete with big agricultural players.

But an area where rainwater is plentiful allows you to collect all the water you need. Rainwater collection and filtration systems are easy and relatively inexpensive to set up, and don’t require massive amounts of electricity to pump.

If you collect rainwater off the roof of a 36x36 foot barn, just one inch of rainfall yields 808 gallons of water.

In a dry place like Phoenix, Arizona, where it only rains about 9 inches per year, you could collect 7,272 gallons of water from that barn. That’s only about 20 gallons per day, and according to the EPA, the average American family uses 300 gallons per day.

Few places on earth supply the 135 inches of rain it would take to meet that capacity from one 36x36 foot barn — and you probably wouldn’t want to live in such a wet place anyway.

You’ll need a larger surface area to collect rainwater. Or you could be more creative with water usage — such as re-using gray water (from showers and washing dishes) to flush toilets and water gardens.

Here is a map that shows the average rainfall per year across the world.

Click on “Total Annual Precipitation” under the “Layers” menu on the left to toggle the legend which shows the number of millimeters of rainfall (1 inch = 25.4 mm) that corresponds to each color on the map.

Under the legend, you can also change the transparency of the map, in order to see cities and borders to better match the data to a specific region.

The map uses data from a 2005 study of average yearly rainfall from 1950-2000, but we have confirmed that it is still accurate by cross referencing recent rainfall data on specific cities from across the globe.

Another map uses data from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration to show a Vegetation Condition Index, as of August 1, 2021. This is a good way to visualize both water availability and soil fertility in one map.

But keep in mind it is summertime in the Northern Hemisphere — obviously you should take winter into account when choosing land.

You can make homesteading work in places with a long winter. It just requires more planning.

But regular sunshine all year long sure makes things a lot easier — when it comes to constant available fresh vegetables, and consistent electricity from solar panels.

Then again, there are other issues to consider, like disease carrying insects, and the types of food you want to grow.

These maps should help give you a broad understanding of what is possible, wherever you want to consider living off the land.
