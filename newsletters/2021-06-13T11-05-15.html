Is Bitcoin-friendly El Salvador a viable living option?

What happened:

You may have heard the news by now that El Salvador has become the first country to officially adopt Bitcoin as legal tender.

This week the young President of El Salvador, Nayib Bukele, sent a bill to the nation’s legislature which was overwhelmingly approved by lawmakers.

The US dollar is still official currency in El Salvador, as it has been since 2001. But now businesses must accept Bitcoin for payment, when offered, if they have the ability.

This also means that if Salvadorans hold Bitcoin and it rises in value, they will not be charged capital gains tax when they sell or spend it, because Bitcoin will be treated as a currency and not a security.

According to the World Bank about $6 billion each year, roughly one fifth of El Salvador’s entire gross domestic product, comes from people outside of El Salvador sending money back home to relatives.

The theory is that Bitcoin will help facilitate these cross border transactions which can be tricky and time consuming using typical banks and the US dollar.

El Salvador also hopes to attract foreign investors by offering them permanent residency (and no capital gains tax) in exchange for investing Bitcoin into the country’s economy.

What this means:

This is exciting news in the march towards legitimizing and promoting the benefits and uses of cryptocurrency.

El Salvador gets credit and attention for being the first country to officially declare Bitcoin legal tender. But it certainly won’t be the last.

Already politicians from Panama and Paraguay have said they will push similar changes in their own countries, on the heels of El Salvador.

Panama has been a popular Plan B destination for Sovereign Man for years. But Paraguay and El Salvador aren’t exactly first world countries...

There is more incentive for poorer nations to attempt to attract some crypto-wealth by getting ahead of the curve and showing they are friendly to cryptocurrency investors, speculators, and traders.

As we touched on last week, smaller countries are in a better position than ever to attract wealth by offering certain perks to relocate there.

But is El Salvador really a viable living option?

What you can do about it:

First off, as great as it would be to pay no capital gains tax on Bitcoin gains, this benefit is not available to US citizens.

That’s because the US taxes its citizens no matter where they live in the world. And while you don’t have to pay taxes on the first $108,700 you earn if you use the Foreign Earned Income Exemption, this does not apply to investment income.

The US territory of Puerto Rico remains US citizens’ best option when it comes to saving money on capital gains tax. Using Act 60 (formerly called Act 22) bonafide residents of Puerto Rico can pay 0% on capital gains accrued after moving to the island. There are other costs however, including a mandatory $10,000 per year donation to an approved Puerto Rican charity.

The only other way for Americans to take full advantage of low and no tax jurisdictions is to renounce their citizenship. But even this could trigger a high exit tax bill. Starting at a net worth of $2 million, the US government will treat you as if you sold everything the day you renounce, and any capital gains over $600,000 will be taxed.

But anyone who is not an American citizen CAN take advantage of low tax jurisdictions by moving there.

So again, the question is, do you want to live in El Salvador?

El Salvador could be considered the most dangerous country in the world. The murder rate in El Salvador is 52 per 100,000 residents, according to World Bank data.

For comparison, the murder rate in Mexico is 29, and in Puerto Rico it is 21 per 100,000.

The murder rate across the entire US is about 5, and in Chile it is 4.4 per 100,000.

Expats report that even in touristy beach towns, it is not safe to walk around after dark.

El Salvador has a major problem with gangs and organized crime, often involved in drug trafficking. But because about a quarter of the population lives in poverty, property crimes are also a huge problem.

It is worth noting that expats in El Salvador say that most crime is confined to gang related activities, and very specific areas of big cities. If you live in a gated community, you might not have much to worry about.

But crime isn’t the only drawback. The healthcare situation is not great. For major issues, even emergencies, you will need to leave the country.

Internet speed is lacking— average download speeds are 23 Mbps according to Ookla Speedtests, which ranks El Salvador behind 121 other countries on internet speed. (Mexico’s internet is, on average, more than twice as fast, and the world average is 102 Mbps.)

El Salvador does have a relatively low costs of living. But so does Mexico, which does not require an investment in order to gain permanent residency.

Even super safe Portugal has a lower cost of living compared to the US, offers easy residency, AND Portugal does not tax capital gains on buying or selling cryptocurrency.

So the question is, why would anyone choose El Salvador when there are better options available?

Again, we’re not bashing El Salvador’s efforts. From the dangerous, impoverished country’s perspective, it makes sense to try whatever it takes to attract investment.

But from a Plan B perspective, there’s no reason to jump into the deep end. Wait and see what happens. Maybe in a few years, El Salvador will be one of many, safe, prosperous countries vying for your business.

The more jurisdictions which treat their citizens and residents as valued customers the better. That’s an exciting development we can look forward to.

And yes, we can thank El Salvador for being part of the movement in that direction.
