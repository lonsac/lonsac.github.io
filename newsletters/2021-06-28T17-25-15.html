​The Three Kinds of Writers​

Novice writers try to sound smart, professional writers try to write clearly, and masters try to communicate emotions that stir the reader's soul.

— —

Writing Letters to Yourself

I’ve started writing letters to myself before making important business decisions. Most of them are 500-1,000 words. In them, I outline my reasoning and lay out the best counter-arguments to my plan.

The original idea came from Amazon’s culture of writing memos. If memos at Amazon help employees make decisions, why shouldn’t we follow the same process for ourselves?

Writing effectively raises your intelligence by outsourcing your working memory to the page. It’s a little like math class, where teachers rightly tell students to put their ideas on scrap paper. They know that the more you write things down, the more your mind can tackle hard problems. By writing, you outsource your working memory which helps you explore nuance in a way that you can’t do with only your mind.

Once some of the challenging decisions that I’m working through right now are behind me, I’ll share some of them publicly.

Instinct vs. Emotion

Writing these letters has taught me the difference between instinct and emotion.

I want to act with more instinct and less emotion. We tend to think they’re the same, but they’re actually the obverse of each other (a fancy way of saying that two things that people think are synonyms are actually antonyms).

Instinct moves with the wisdom of evolution. It goes beyond the limits of language and encompasses aspects of life beyond what can be described in words. But emotion is the opposite. As the voice of hormones and environmental factors, it cares only about the present.

The Buck Stops Here

While we’re talking about language, let's talk about the origins of this phrase. It was popularized by US President Harry Truman, who kept a sign with the phrase on his desk in the White House.

“Buck passing” was originally a term from poker. In the early days of poker, the buck referred to the person who was dealing the cards. Players who didn’t want to deal could pass the buck to somebody else. But on a number of occasions, Truman said “the buck stops here” to take responsibility and confirm that ultimate responsibility stopped with him.

Writing Like a Lion

Great writers are like lions.

Even though they’re kings of the safari, lions spend most of their time sleeping and lounging around. But when opportunity strikes, they become beasts who chase after their goals with 100% focus.

Writers should operate similarly. They should spend most of their time away from the keyboard — where they can read, experience the world, and talk with friends. But when it’s time to write, they should turn on Lion’s Mode. They should set a timer for how long they want to write. Until it goes off, they should be exclusively focused on the task at hand and tune out all distractions.

The average writer does the opposite. They are far too distracted when they write and spend way too long writing. Like a lion, it’s generally better to work in shorter sprints.

Teachers and the Limits of Prescriptions

The more I study what makes people successful, the more I realize that prescriptions cannot provide an ultimate answer. Articles like “7 Ways to Improve your Writing” may be attractive, but they will never help you become a world-class writer. At some level, everybody knows this. True excellence is beyond words. It happens at an intuitive level, beyond the limits of language.

But if prescriptions can’t make you great, aren’t teachers useless? And if they can’t provide ultimate answers, why should we learn from them?

Teachers can give you the map, but not the territory. We all know that some maps are better than others. Remember, for example, when Google Maps was significantly better than Apple Maps? In the early days, Apple Maps provided an incorrect address for Washington Dulles Airport and according to CNN, the directions "could get a driver arrested and possibly run over by a 747.” Yikes. A bad teacher will similarly steer you in the wrong direction.

Good teachers are the opposite. We follow them for their maps, even if they don’t tell the whole story. Everybody has looked at an accurate map of a city before arriving and thought: “This isn’t what I expected.” That’s because a map cannot paint a complete picture, no matter how accurate it is.

You have to experience the territory for yourself. When you do, you develop a kind of intuitive knowledge that you are not even conscious of. It’s like Polanyi’s Paradox: “We know more than we think we know.” Though you have to find these ultimate answers for yourself, a good teacher speeds up the learning process by giving you a useful map to start with.
