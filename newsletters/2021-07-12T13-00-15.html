The Iceberg

Your ideas are like an iceberg.

Some are easy to find, but most are hiding deep in your mind, waiting for the pen to discover them.

Creative Schedules

The ideal creative schedule: Short bursts of extreme intensity, surrounded by long periods of chill.

The challenge is that the calendars of successful creatives don’t look like this. They have meetings to attend and employees to manage, which is why creatives often feel like victims of their own success.

As Naval Ravikant says: "Creativity starts with an empty calendar and ends with a full one.”

Fighting this entropy is a challenge for anybody who does creative work. And ultimately, if you want to raise creative kids, resist the urge to over-schedule their lives.

The Tetris Blocks of Writing

If you want to write consistently, it helps to think of your intellectual life as a game of Tetris.

You collect notes when you read and experiences whenever you leave the house. Eventually, you find ways to put them together and create quality writing.

The main difference between writing and Tetris is that you don’t use the vast majority of what you collect. Expression is compression. Just as it takes 50 gallons of sap to make one gallon of maple syrup, you have to collect an arsenal of ideas before you can distill them into quality writing.

The Wind and the Current

There’s no epiphany quite like the epiphany of instantly knowing an idea is going to become a permanent part of your worldview when you read it.

That’s what Seth Godin gifted me over the weekend when he wrote The Current and the Wind.

He wrote: "The wind gets all the attention. The wind howls and the wind gusts… But the wind is light. The current, on the other hand is persistent and heavy. On a river, it’s the current that will move the canoe far more than the wind will. But the wind distracts us.”

When you can, ignore the wind. Study the current instead.

I suspect that people have similar winds but vastly different currents. Let’s test that theory. In my life, here are examples of both:

Wind:

  • Drama in my personal life
  • 99.99% of breaking news
  • Books and articles about current events
  • Hype-driven trends

​
Current:

  • Deepening relationships with family and my closest friends
  • Cultivating knowledge by traveling and studying the Western canon
  • Living the Write of Passage life by writing long-form essays, having deep conversations, and growing my email list
  • Transitioning Write of Passage from a course to a company that’s enjoyable to run and can thrive without my day-to-day involvement
  • Deepening my Business Model Flywheel​

Free Will

I spent the last month living with a performance coach named Chris Sparks.

If there’s an idea of his that stuck with me, it’s this: "When it comes to creating your environment, assume you have free will. When it comes to living in it, assume you have no free will.”

Here’s an example: I lost my love of working out during the pandemic. Before it started, I worked out at a nice, high-energy gym in New York, where I was motivated by committed people with beautiful bodies. The closing of gyms forced me to work out at home. Within months, I dreaded my daily workout.

When I moved to Austin, I lifted at a basic local gym. An average person would have called it heaven because it was so empty. Some days, I was the only person there. But my mind and body were disconnected. No matter how much my mind wanted me to be fit, my body couldn’t find the energy to push itself.

I escaped my slump when I arrived in Oaxaca. I found the most intense gym in town. It was a converted warehouse with squat racks, more machines than I knew what to do with, and larger-than-life photos of bodybuilders on the walls. Everybody I saw was committed to sculpting their bodies into a beautiful shape. The energy was intoxicating. In my life, I’ve never seen a higher percentage of people sweating at the gym. Without realizing it, I rediscovered a love for working out that I lost during the pandemic. I read Arnold Schwarzenegger’s book: The Education of a Bodybuilder. Now I’m reading his biography.

When inhabiting my environment, I assume I don't have free will. I assume I'm a lazy bum who will copy everybody else around me and follow the path of least resistance.

But at the same time, I have the free will to design that environment.

I accept that I’ll never be 100% self-determined. Not even close. I'll become just like the people I surround myself with. Instead of fighting my nature, I lean into it. Like a glass of cold water that warms up to room temperature when it's placed on the table, I know that the pull of entropy makes me act like my surroundings. I have a finite amount of motivational energy. Instead of mustering the will to push myself on my own, I use my free will to architect environments where I can accomplish my goals by being in sync with my surroundings.

And this morning, I joined a new gym in Austin.
