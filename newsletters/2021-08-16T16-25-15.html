Coolest Things I Learned This Week

​Imitate, then Innovate​

Ironically, the more we imitate others, the more we discover our unique style.

There’s a long lineage of comedians who tried to copy each other, failed, and became great themselves: Johnny Carson tried to copy Jack Benny, but failed and won six Emmy awards. Then, David Letterman tried to copy Johnny Carson, but failed and became one of America’s great television hosts.

Reflecting on his own influences, Conan O’Brien said: “It is our failure to become our perceived ideal that ultimately defines us and makes us unique.”

All these comedians learned that imitation reveals our identity, especially when we fall short of those we admire. To improve your writing, binge-read your favorite writers and imitate their style when you write.

Aim for perfection but pay attention to your shortcomings, for they hold the seeds of your individuality.

Sherlock Holmes, on Saving Ideas

Note-taking unlocked my ability to write consistently, which is why I teach it at the start of Write of Passage. Many students save too many ideas though. When they do, their note-taking system loses value because there’s too much junk.

You only want to save the top 5-10% of information. Otherwise, you’re going to clutter your mind.

For answers, I’ve turned to Sherlock Holmes.
​
He thinks of his brain like a little empty attic. You have to be careful with the furniture you store there. Fools store everything they come across up there, which crowds out useful possessions that should be easy to find. Skillful workmen, in contrast, are very careful about what they let into their brain-attic. They have nothing but the tools that’ll help them do their work, and they keep them in perfect order.

Holmes says: “It is of the highest importance, therefore, not to have useless facts elbowing out the useful ones.”

The Wright Brothers: Intellectual Curiosity

I’ve been pouring through David McCullough’s biography of the Wright Brothers. As obsessive learners and voracious writers, their philosophy was in tune with the spirit of this newsletter.

They grew up in a home full of books because their father was a reading-obsessed preacher. He valued school less than informal home education. He let the boys skip school to read or work on projects at home.

One time, the Wright Brothers were praised for being so successful despite having no special advantages in the world. To that, Orville disagreed. Reflecting on his childhood, he said: "The greatest thing in our favor was growing up in a family that encouraged intellectual curiosity.”

The Wright Brothers: Surprising Similarities

The Wright Brothers are known for their aviation prowess, but they started as bicycle repairmen. They owned a shop in their native town of Dayton, Ohio. As one friend said of them: “Bring up the subject of the shapes of handlebars or types of pedals on early ‘safety bicycles’ and his whole face lights up.”

Later, they applied intellectual curiosity to building airplanes. From bicycles, they learned to build strong but lightweight structures. Like a bicycle, balance was central to early airplanes. In the Wright Brothers’ early design, the pilots turned the airplane by shifting their balance. Rolling right or left caused the airplane to turn, similar to the way you turn on a bicycle.

Strong Personal Monopolies are often born out of these "surprising similarities,” where people apply lessons from one field to another. Distinct projects can be surprisingly similar.

Today, my favorite example is Rich Barton, the founder of Expedia, Zillow, and Glassdoor — a $21 billion travel company, a $23 billion real estate company, and a $1.6 billion recruiting company. But as Kevin Kwok shows in this article, he used the same philosophy to build all three companies. All three industries were clouded by information asymmetries. He used the Internet to transfer power from incumbents to ordinary people: "Glassdoor revealed how employees really felt about companies. Zillow shed light on what any house was worth. Expedia let people see the prices and availability of flights and hotels without talking to an agent.”

From Barton and the Wright Brothers, we see how one way of thinking can apply to various fields. What looks like an insane leap to others can feel like a small step to you.

Photo of the Week

Speaking of the Wright Brothers, I once visited the site of their first flight.

One of my college friends was a pilot who needed to get his hours up. On Valentine’s Day of our senior year, we surprised our girlfriends and flew across the state of North Carolina. We landed at Kitty Hawk airport, parked the plane, and saw the site for ourselves.

In the second photo, I’m standing next to a replica of the original Wright flyer. If you zoom in, you can see how the brothers used to lie down on the airplane and imagine how the plane would turn whenever they shifted their bodies.
