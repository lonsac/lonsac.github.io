Banks, QE, and Money-Printing
Lyn Alden

A well-written explanation of exactly what "money printing" means. People love the "Money Printer goes BRRR" meme, but as Lyn points out in this piece, it’s possible for "Money Printer to go BRR" to the tune of trillions of dollars without ever creating inflation because of how money flows around the banking system.

Here’s the key point:

When the Fed does QE [Quantitative Easing] alone, it increases bank reserves in the system, but doesn’t necessarily increase the broad money supply.

The broad money supply only increases when either

a) banks create more money by making loans or

b) the federal government runs big fiscal deficits to inject money to people and businesses in the system.

Many people miss that second one; they assume that money supply can only increase if banks lend, which isn’t correct.

Effectively, when the Federal Reserve "prints money," that money goes into the banking system and so the only way it gets into the broader economy is through those banks making loans. If that money goes to banks but they don’t lend much of it out (as happened in the wake of 2008) then you just get a better capitalized banking system and no consumer price inflation.

In order for consumer price inflation to occur, consumers need to get that money. The PPP and similar unemployment benefits extended in many countries around the world in response to COVID is one way that can happen (AKA fiscal stimulus). That’s not to say it’s guaranteed to cause sustained inflation, it may not, but it is a bare minimum precondition.

The Kaoboy of Convertible Arb With Michael Kao
RCM Alternatives

A convertible bond is a bond that converts to equity at some strike price. If you were to model it in options terms then, a convertible bond is like being long a call (if the price goes up, you get equity shares like you would owning a call option) and short a put (if the price goes down and the company goes bankrupt, you have all the downside risk of selling a put).

This is a kind of esoteric instrument for most people, but the crypto crowd may want to pay attention.

Michael Saylor’s firm Microstrategy, which has converted all its excess cash into bitcoin (and pledged to commit any future cash flows into bitcoin), also has issued a lot of convertible bonds.

As Michael explained in a Twitter thread:

Between his 2 convertible bonds, he has effectively embedded a $1.7B SHORT PUT position on his company’s enterprise value.

...

If BTC continues its ascent and MSTR convertibles wind up being converted to equity, Saylor will be GOAT.

...

If, on the other hand, some exogenous event (like the scenario I laid out) makes MSTR's [assets less than liabilities] when his [liabilities] comes due, you will witness a spectacular capital structure implosion.
