Coolest Things I Learned This Week

Make Something Out of What You Read

The philosopher Giambattista Vico once said: "We only know what we make.”

This idea is the driving force behind my intellectual life.

I try to create something out of every book I read. The Internet is filled with full book summaries, so instead of talking about the entire thing, I explore one idea deeply. With Arnold Schwarzenegger, I’m making a YouTube video about his marketing philosophy. He said: "No matter what you do in life, selling is part of it.” He didn’t just become the world’s best bodybuilder. He promoted the sport itself and persuaded people to take fitness seriously. And he didn’t just film movies. He was one of the first actors to promote films with global speaking tours and late-night talk show appearances.

With da Vinci, I’m going to challenge the idea that the best creatives aren’t in it for the money. As I wrote in a recent Musings, da Vinci began an apprenticeship at the age of 14, where he studied under Andrea del Verrocchio, who ran one of the best workshops in Florence. The shop was driven by profit as much as creative excellence. As Isaacson writes: The goal of the shop was to "produce a constant flow of marketable art and artifacts rather than nurture creative geniuses yearning to find outsets for their originality."

When da Vinci arrived in Florence, there were at least 100 “very wealthy” families and a prosperous middle class. As Isaacson writes, many of them had to assert their social status because they were new to wealth. Among other things, they did so by commissioning works of art. Most famously, the Medici family was based in Florence and one of the wealthiest families in Europe. After Cosimo de’ Medici took over the family bank in 1430, he became a patron of Renaissance art. His grandson, Lorenzo de’ Medici, sponsored artists like Botticelli and Michelangelo. He also funded plays, carnivals, and paintings which inspired Leonardo da Vinci.

​Fragile Ideas​

At Steve Jobs’ memorial service, Jony Ive said: “I think he better than anyone understood that while ideas ultimately can be so powerful, they begin as fragile, barely formed thoughts, so easily missed, so easily compromised, so easily squished.”

This is exactly right. New ideas need to be protected like a bird protects their eggs. When they’re first born, they’re weak. Even the smallest pieces of criticism can kill them.

Shelter your young ideas, let them grow, and eventually, they’ll be able to fly.

​Arizona State University​

Pop quiz: What American university is innovating in the most impressive ways right now?

Here’s an answer you might not expect: Arizona State University. And yeah, I’m talking about the Arizona party school. The university appointed a new president in 2002. Instead of following the Ivy League playbook and building a name through exclusivity, ASU did the opposite and asked: “How many students can we serve every year?”

This metaphor stuck out in particular. As Nadia Eghbal writes: "Borrowing a restaurant metaphor from Harvard physician Atul Gawande, Crow and Dabars explain that most universities aspire towards offering a Michelin star experience to students, but what we actually need is a ‘fast casual’, Cheesecake Factory-like option that can provide an affordable, quality education to millions.”

Today, ASU admits 86 percent of applicants while Harvard admits only 5 percent. They focused on recruiting out-of-state students who pay higher tuition than in-state ones.

Taken all together, the ASU flywheel is simple: more students mean more resources, more resources mean better results, and better results mean more students.

​Western Storytelling​

Camille Paglia argues that when it comes to storytelling, tragedy is a uniquely Western invention. The genre gave us the kinds of rise and falls that peak with a climax.

Traditional eastern stories, she says, don’t have the same levels of suspense or the sense of an ending.

Western narratives are different. They have moments of extreme intensity, which are also reflected in Western classical music. Aristotle called this moment of maximum intensity: peripeteia — which translates to reversal.

Photo of the Week

That’s the bookshelf in my living room, which I’ll talk more about in a second.

​Tyler Cowen recently wrote about the inspirations for his excellent Conversations with Tyler podcast. I love how random and disjointed they are:

  • Monty Python’s Flying Circus (especially the interview segments, which maintain remarkable dramatic tension)
  • Seinfeld (wonderful ensemble work)
  • Curb Your Enthusiasm (how long can you keep people on edge?)
  • The TNT halftime show with Charles Barkley, Kenny Smith, etc. (what makes for a dramatic or memorable interjection?).

The point isn’t that you should copy Tyler’s influences. Rather, if you want to do interesting creative work, you should ask: “How do my influences differ from people who do work like mine?”

Here’s my answer to that question: There are almost no books about writing on my bookshelf. Strategically, my biggest inspirations are people like Lewis Mumford and Marshall McLuhan, who developed the principles of media theory more than 50 years ago. Rather than reading books about writing style, I subscribe to a philosophy of Imitate, then Innovate. I read books like Shantaram and copy the things I like about their writing style.

Though I don’t always succeed, I aim to skip the books that everybody else in my field is reading and cultivate a quirky blend of influences instead.
