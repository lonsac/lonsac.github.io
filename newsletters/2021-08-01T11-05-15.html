Your “Right to Food”: The Best Places to Pursue Self-Sufficiency

What happened:

Maine voters will choose in a referendum vote on November 2, whether or not to add an amendment to the state’s constitution that guarantees the “right to food.”

But the language of the amendment does NOT say people have the right to be provided with food.

Rather, it would enshrine the right to grow your own food.

The amendment reads:

“All individuals have a natural, inherent and unalienable right to food, including the right to save and exchange seeds and the right to grow, raise, harvest, produce and consume the food of their own choosing for their own nourishment, sustenance, bodily health and well-being, as long as an individual does not commit trespassing, theft, poaching or other abuses of private property rights, public lands or natural resources in the harvesting, production or acquisition of food.”

While this amendment may seem unimportant today, supporters say it is forward thinking to nip in the bud any future government restrictions on self sufficiency — perhaps influenced by large corporate food producers.

What this means:

Anyone familiar with the difference between positive and negative rights may have rolled their eyes when they saw a push for the “right to food.”

Positive rights are things that must be provided. Saying you have the right to healthcare, for example, means someone must provide you with healthcare.

Negative rights are things that exist in nature. You have the right to life, liberty, and property, because you can live freely and create wealth as long as no one infringes on your right to do so.

Maine’s version of a “right to food” is a negative right. It doesn’t say you have the positive right for someone else to provide you with food— which would imply higher taxes, more bureaucracy, or even forced labor.

In other words, the government does not have the authority to stop you from growing your own food.

What you can do about it:

Last week we talked about the most resilient countries against food shortages, when it comes to how much food they currently produce and are capable of growing.

This week we’ll cover the best places for food self sufficiency, where you can homestead and grow your own food with minimal restrictions.

The United States

The US is a big place, with major differences by state and region on land and water use rights.

Unincorporated land outside of town limits will generally give you more freedom to do whatever you want on your own land — construct outbuildings, raise livestock, etc. We talked about this in a previous Sunday Intelligence article.

You’ll still need to research individual county ordinances before you buy land to see what kind of restrictions they impose — for example, some people have to build “chicken trailers” on wheels to avoid permitting requirements for permanent structures.

It is legal in every state to collect rainwater on your own property — some even encourage it. But others have restrictions. For example, Colorado limits you to two barrels with a maximum capacity of 110 gallons.

Maine, North Dakota, Utah, and Wyoming each have food freedom laws which to varying degrees exempt homesteaders from licensing regulations about selling home produced food.

In a June 2020 Sovereign Man: Confidential Alert on taking back control of your food supply, we identified a number of suitable spots to homestead.

We left out places like California that may have fertile land, but are clearly lacking in freedom.

Southern Missouri, in the Ozarks, made the cut because in additional to abundant rainfall and good land, the homesteading culture is engrained in the area. The risk of random politicians or neighbors campaigning to limit the food and livestock you can grow on your own land is low.

Eastern Texas is another good pick, for similar reasons. It rains in this area, and you have access to deep aquifers. But the closer you get to cities like Austin, the more likely you are to run into restrictions on land use.

Alaska also shows potential, as strange as that may seem in a place where it’s dark half the year. But the 20+ hours of strong daylight all summer make the three month growing season very productive. There is also abundant forage and hunting opportunities. Plus the sheer size means you can easily find a place to be left alone.

Outside the US

Just like in the United States, land use rights in most countries are governed locally. As a foreigner moving to the country, the first step (after obtaining residency) is understanding if you are allowed to own land.

In Mexico, for example, foreigners are generally restricted from owning land within 30 miles of the coast, although there are ways to work around this using a trust. Interior land, however, can be owned outright by foreigners. With it being cheap and abundant, this is likely where you would want to set up a homestead in Mexico anyway.

It is also legal to harvest rainwater in Mexico, and there has actually been a growing movement lately encouraging more adoption of rainwater collection for certain stressed water systems in the country.

In Portugal, buying land is actually one of the ways to obtain residency through its Golden Visa program. The investment can be as little as €280,000 (~$332,000) if you purchase a property that is in need of renovation and located in an area with a low population density — the type of place you might want for a homestead anyway.

It is also legal — though regulated — to harvest rainwater in Portugal.

You can learn more about the Portuguese Golden Visa program in your Starter Guide.

Once you know you can own land, and use the water that falls on it, it is a matter of finding unrestricted land based on what you want to do with it, such as constructing outbuildings and raising livestock.

For that, it is worth hiring a reputable local attorney or real estate expert familiar with that particular country’s rules. For something as important as investing your time and money in a homestead, it makes sense to hire a professional to understand your land rights.
