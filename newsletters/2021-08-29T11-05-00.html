The Most Friendly Destinations for Cryptocurrency

What happened:

Which is the most friendly Bitcoin destination?

1. Cool Valley, Missouri is a town of about 1,500 people who might soon all own Bitcoin.

The Mayor of Cool Valley wants to give every resident $1,000 worth of Bitcoin, but not allow them to touch it for a period of about five years. He thinks that Bitcoin will continue its rise in value, and by then, the gains would be transformational for the people of his town.

He claims to have secured private donors willing to match funds the mayor raises for the initiative.

2. Switzerland now has 85,000 retail locations that accept Bitcoin and Ether.

A French payment processor called Worldline has teamed up with a crypto-financial services company called Bitcoin Suisse. Any merchant using Worldline’s payment processing can now accept Bitcoin and Ether in store and online.

The cryptocurrencies are then immediately converted into Swiss Francs for the merchant in order to avoid price volatility issues.

3. Sweden just returned 33 Bitcoin to a drug dealer upon his release from prison.

When the man was convicted, prosecutors did not argue that he should forfeit the 36 Bitcoin he accrued from selling drugs. Instead, they argued he should forfeit the value of the Bitcoin at the time in Swedish Krona.

That came out to about $136,000. But today the coins are worth closer to $1.7 million.

Since the government hadn’t yet auctioned off the Bitcoin, the courts said they could only auction the Bitcoin equivalent of $136,000, leaving about 33 Bitcoin to be returned to the drug dealer.

What this means:

These anecdotal stories represent some of the hype and potential around Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies.

Will it work as a long term savings or retirement plan? Can it replace traditional currencies for marketplace transactions?

Or is it just a get rich quick scheme, or a useful way for criminals to sell contraband?

Clearly at Sovereign Man we see the world-changing potential of cryptocurrencies. But we also take a rational approach.

Cryptocurrencies are too new to know exactly what will happen — to rely solely on them for a store of value or to predict accurately which, if any, will be widely accepted as money in the future.

But it makes perfect sense to give yourself some exposure to such an exciting new technology with so much potential.

However governments don’t always share that enthusiasm for new technology — especially one that threatens its main pillar of power, central banking.

What you can do about it:

Here’s what you need to know about how different countries treat your crypto for tax purposes.

United States

For US federal tax purposes, the IRS has stated that virtual currency is treated as property.

Cryptocurrency traders and speculators have an obligation to pay capital gains tax on every trade or transaction, including trading one cryptocurrency for another cryptocurrency.

If you buy a $5 latte with an amount of Bitcoin you acquired for $1, you owe capital gains tax on the $4 gain. Obviously, that creates quite an obstacle for actually using crytpocurrency as money.

As for cryptocurrency exchanges, they must comply with the same record-keeping and suspicious transaction reporting requirements as typical banks and other financial companies dealing in fiat currencies.

The United States taxes citizens no matter where they live in the world, so apart from renouncing US citizenship (which itself could trigger taxes on cryptos), there are limited options to reduce your capital gains tax.

Puerto Rico provides an exception.

Because of Act 60 tax incentives, US citizens who become bona fide residents of Puerto Rico and take advantage of the Individual Investor Act (formerly Act 22) can pay 0% capital gains on most investment income.

But the key is that this only applies to gains accrued after becoming a bona fide resident.

So about that $5 latte we talked about earlier...

Say you bought the Bitcoin for $1 and moved to the island when it was worth $3. Then the value increases enough to buy the $5 coffee.

You will owe capital gains tax to the US government on the gain from $1 to $3. But you will not owe capital gains tax on the gain from $3 to $5, because it happened after you became a resident of Puerto Rico.

Canada

Canada considers cryptocurrency a “commodity.”

That means, whether using cryptocurrency as a unit of payment, receiving crypto as a salary or wage, or trading crypto, you must report cryptocurrency profits as income and pay the appropriate tax.

Also, if you transact with cryptocurrency, Goods and Service Tax (GST) applies on the fair market value of the purchase of these goods or services.

Portugal

Cryptocurrencies are not considered legal tender in Portugal. But, according to the Portuguese Tax & Customs Authority (PTA), Portugal treats cryptocurrencies as a form of currency.

That means profits on cryptocurrencies are not a taxable event.

In 2016, the PTA clarified this tax decision and carved out two exceptions: If you’re 1) dealing in cryptocurrencies as a professional or business activity, then you’re subject to regular Portuguese income tax rates (ranging from 14.5% to 48%), and if you’re 2) exchanging cryptocurrency for goods or services, then an invoice should be issued and the service should be taxed.

But as an individual cryptocurrency holder or trader, you’ll be tax-free. Further, Portugal does not levy VAT (Value Added Tax) on cryptocurrency transactions.

So, for now, Portugal is a very favorable environment for cryptocurrency traders and holders.

Malta

Cryptocurrency transactions are treated the same way as transactions involving conventional currency. That means cryptocurrency profits are not subject to taxes — no income tax, no capital gains tax.

Further, you won’t face VAT at cryptocurrency exchanges, creating a very favorable environment for cryptocurrency traders and holders.

This applies to what Malta considers “coins”— cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin and Ethereum designed solely as a means of payment, medium of exchange or a store of value.

“Tokens” which serve as a security, representing ownership in a company or other asset, are treated differently.

If you’re not a US citizen, of course, you have plenty of other options as well. Being a tax resident of any country which does not tax capital gains should protect your crypto profits and help you avoid reporting requirements when you spend it.
