Risk Parity: Why We Lever
AQR

When an investor wants to increase the expected returns of their portfolio, there are only two ways to do it and they both involve risk.

One is to take "concentration risk" - that is to shift the allocation of the portfolio to be more concentrated and aggressive overall. This usually looks like a higher percentage of assets in equities (often tech and crypto for most people today). These assets generally seem to have higher returns and higher risk so adding more of them should increase both the return and the risk of the portfolio.

The other way is to take "leverage risk." That is, to keep using an overall balanced portfolio but to add mild to moderate leverage at the portfolio level (NOT at the individual asset level).

The former, allocation risk, is what most investors do in practice. In my opinion, this is generally a mistake. As Cliff Asness notes:

All else equal, more leverage increases both risk and expected return. But all else is not equal here. The willingness to use modest leverage allows a risk parity investor to build a more diversified, more balanced, higher-return-for-the-risk-taken portfolio. In our view, this more than compensates risk parity investors for the necessity of employing some leverage.

I have never heard anyone make a particularly compelling argument against this observation and yet few investors do it. In part, I think this is logistical. It’s hard for most non-institutional investors to apply portfolio leverage.

But, I think the bigger part is that most people grew up in households where there was a general feeling that "all leverage is bad."

I am sympathetic to this view and I would agree that "most leverage is bad" because most people leverage individual assets rather than diversified portfolios.

There is no remotely reasonable argument for why it’s prudent to go leverage your crypto exposure 10x on margin. If you want to do it because you enjoy gambling, go for it.

Ultimately, I think leveraging a highly diversified portfolio of global stocks, bonds, commodities, volatility strategies (and perhaps other assets) is a better alternative to the investor seeking more yield than concentrating on riskier assets.

Robert Sapolsky, Ph.D.: The pervasive effect of stress – is it killing you?
 Peter Attia

Robert Sapolsky has spent most of his ~40-year career studying stress and its impacts. He spent 30 of those years observing stress in a baboon colony in East Africa.

This was a wide-ranging and tour de force interview in my opinion and I can’t possibly summarize it, but here were some of the best bits:

There is no real qualitative difference between the neurons of a fruit fly and the neurons of a human, it is the quantitative difference at the micro-level (neurons) which causes the qualitative differences at the macro level! An amazing example of how complex systems exhibit emergence!

Sapolsky: You know, we're sharing 98% of our DNA with a chimp or a bonobo. So you say we're human this come from and I think it's exactly the issue talking about we have the same signal transaction pathways and neurons as you see in a fruit fly. And what's the key difference?

Attia: Isn't that a bit humbling?

Sapolsky: Yes. When we just think we're that special and it's where you can take one of their genes or one of our genes and stick it in the other one and it functions just fine. Or you could take it from a single cell organism. Some genes related to programmed cell death. You can do that.

So what's the big difference for every neuron that a fruit fly has?

We have 100 million. And sort of the sound bite that I think summarizes all of that is with enough quantity. You invent quality and this is this whole world of emergent properties of complex systems. You take one ant and you put it on a table and nothing that it does makes a huge amount of sense and you put 10 ants there and pretty much the same thing and you put on a no 1000 and maybe they start making a trail or something and you put 10,000 and they build a colony and they far mushrooms and they take aphids as slaves and they could keep the temperature in the colony plus or minus two degrees and they have specialization of labor and no aunt has any more rules than he had when he was watering around on the table by himself. And you put enough of them together and complex adaptive stuff emerges out of the very simple rules that each of those components has for dealing with another component.

On the importance of social status to happiness and health outcomes.

Sapolsky: One of the most interesting things about us as humans, when we talk about all sorts of primates, being low, ranking in general is bad for your health or what about humans? We don't have ranking in the same system that sort of other primates do in a strict linear kind of way. But the other thing about us is we belong to multiple hierarchies at once, and we can have very different ranks in them.

You've got some guy who's the mail room clerk in this giant corporation and you could not possibly be lower status, but he's the captain of the company softball team this year. And you better bet when you ask him what's important in his life, 9-5 Monday to Friday is just a stupid way to pay the rent. And what really matters is when the weekend comes around you have somebody with a horrible low status job, who's the deacon of their church. You have some, we belong to multiple hierarchies and we are very good psychologically at deciding that whichever hierarchy we're highest in, that's the one we define ourselves by.

…

I have a friend who's a cardiologist who splits his time between a hospital in Oakland and a private practice in San Francisco. Some of the time what he's doing is dealing with eight year olds who are considering getting a pacemaker because the ski season is coming and they are wondering if they're going to be in shape in time for that.

Back in Oakland, he's dealing with 50 year old elderly men who are having heart disease. When you look at the source of variability and health among humans on this planet, socioeconomic status differences, differences in absolute levels, differences and degrees of inequality within cultures and within communities and so on.

They're an enormous predictor of health. You want to look at the most medically impacted, low ranking primates you could find on earth - look at poor humans. Because when humans invented socioeconomic status and the capacity to be poor, they invented a way to subordinate the have nots like no chimp on earth could ever dream of doing

On how we often misinterpret our physiological reactions.

Sapolsky: One example of this is just like this classic great study and sort of physiological psychology, social psych you take people and I think in the study they would take male volunteers, heterosexual, male volunteers. And of course there were no doubt college freshmen taking psych 101. And they had these guys walk across this really scary suspended bridge and the deal was that either halfway across the bridge you were met by someone from the other side, a researcher who happened to be an attractive female and she would ask the volunteer some questions.

What do you think of this bridge or how is it walking across this or whatever? The other half of the time you would not encounter this person until you were safely out at the other end. Then they would ask you to evaluate the attractiveness of the person, same person, that same person seeing her in the middle of the bridge or at the far end.

Exactly saying, hey, remember while you were doing that, there was this lady, you know, do you want to rate her attractiveness? Was she friendly? Did she seem smart? And what you would see is guys in the middle of the bridge with their hearts racing because they're terrified would do some misinterpretation. Why is my heart beating so much faster than if I was standing safely?

Attia: So they would rate her more attractively in the middle of the bridge?

Sapolsky: Exactly.

On why you need to do some form of stress management every day.

First off, you can't do your stress management on the weekends kind of thing. That needs to be a regular sort of thing. You can't do it while you're on hold on the phone for 30 seconds. You need to set time out for it. The benefits of aerobic exercise, I don't know what the magic number is these days, about 20 or 30 minute blocks as a minimum to start getting the cardiovascular benefits. So it's got to be something like you stop things to do.

On why just committing to making a change often triggers many of the benefits even before you do anything else.

Sapolsky: That taps into one of the 80/20 rules of sort of mental health professionals. Okay, so you have this crazy stressful lifestyle and there's just a bazillion things you can't say no to and you're just going 24/7. And if you've gotten to the point of saying this is crazy, this is not a quality life, I want to live a healthy quality old age.

Well, I got to get this stuff under control. I'm going to start doing some stress management.

And if you've gotten to the point where in this lifestyle where there are a billion things you can't say no to each day, you're saying no to them enough that nonetheless, 20 minutes every single day you're doing this, it almost doesn't matter what intervention you're doing. You're 80% of the way there already if you are managing to set aside time for anything.

And that's very similar to this sort of amazing classic finding. You get people who are clinically depressed and finally, finally, finally, they're going to do something about it and merely making a first appointment even before you've seen the person, people feel significantly better because you are finally saying I matter enough to do something about this, or I'm activated enough or I'm optimistic enough to actually go.

You're halfway there at that point, merely by doing something on a near-daily basis.

Attia: So just on that logic, even though I don't know that this has been done, you would argue that 10 minutes of deliberate, mindful practice of meditation daily would probably be better than one hour once a week.

Sapolsky: Absolutely. In part because if nothing else, if you do a daily seven times a week, you were sitting in the aftermath of having done that versus only one time a week, and an awful lot of what's most interesting about physiology and its impact on sort of mental health. Things like that are what's happening in the recovery period. After something like that, in the same way, one awful hour-long stressor a week versus punctuated episodes of it throughout the week. Without question. The latter is worse because you've now got umpteen different times that you have to recover from having turned on the stress response and that's where a lot of the damage occurs.
