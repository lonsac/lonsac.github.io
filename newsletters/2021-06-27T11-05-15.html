A Big Career Shakeup Is Coming

What happened:

Obviously the Covid-19 pandemic, and the government lockdowns, caused major changes in the jobs market.

Many low-wage service jobs were cut and replaced with generous stimulus and unemployment benefits.

Now that the government has stopped stifling the economy, these jobs are back— but the workers aren’t. Many are still collecting the more lucrative unemployment benefits.

Therefore, wages are rising at the bottom level of the job market, and employers are trying to boost hiring with sign-on bonuses.

Meanwhile, higher-skilled white collar office workers started working from home, and were in many cases prevented from their typical spending on leisure, travel, and going out.

According to Federal Reserve data, the US savings rate—how much Americans have leftover after each paycheck—jumped from about 7.5% in 2019 to over 16% in 2020. So far in 2021, it’s 18%.

In a FlexJobs survey, 38% of respondents said they are saving $5,000 or more per year by not commuting and by eating lunch at home. And 20% said they are saving $10,000 per year.

Now that some offices are opening back up, people are unwilling to change their routines. Otherwise, they would lose the flexibility, money, and time savings that working from home afforded.

58% of workers say they would look for another job if asked to go back to the office full-time.

With all the extra savings, many people are in a position make good on that. It is giving people the confidence they need to switch jobs, switch careers, or do some extra training to boost their job and pay prospects.

In fact, as many as 9 out of 10 workers pursued some type of skill or professional development during the last year— including 7% earning a new degree, and 28% a new certification.

And the US Bureau of Labor Statistics’ latest release showed the number of people who quit jobs in April climbed to a high of 4 million, including an increase of 94,000 workers in professional and business services, compared to the month before.

What this means:

We’re now starting to get a glimpse of what this means for the future of work trends.

It presents opportunities for everyone: employees, employers, and even unskilled people just starting out.

For employees, it means you can be pickier about taking a job, which could lead to higher pay or a position more tailored to your interests.

It also further frees people to live where they want— 37% of workers said they would definitely consider relocating if they secured a permanent remote job.

For employers, it means you can have the first picks of good employees if you are willing to meet their demands— like allowing remote work, and flexible schedules to accommodate families.

And for the unskilled just starting out, it means a little bit of job training or skills upgrade will go a long way.

What you can do about it:

Some experts say that there just isn’t enough training available in the US... as if it was the government’s role to drop training into workers’ laps, or shove it down their throats.

But the fact is, anyone can find training online for almost anything— and usually it’s free.

For workers: This is a chance to prove yourself— get the training without hand-holding. That shows initiative and gives you more bargaining power.

There are a record high 9.3 million job openings in the US right now.

Web developers, computer systems administrators, coders, and similar tech jobs have some of biggest growth prospects, outside of medical and science fields.

For employers: This is a good litmus test. Did the person you are interviewing seek out and accomplish training themselves? Chances are that will make them a self-directed, valuable employee.

Again, you should be able to find high quality workers if you are willing to give them the flexibility they want.

And if you have trouble finding them in your base country— look elsewhere. Remote work means you have a global pool of talent to choose from, and it could be cheaper to look abroad.

But this also means task-based work is a must. Employers should have clear expectations, deadlines, and deliverables. While this may take more planning and development of processes up front, in the long term, it means requiring fewer middle managers.

This could also be a great time to start a business, become self-employed, or freelance.

E-commerce sales climbed by 3.3X throughout the pandemic, which presents an opportunity for people to go into business for themselves, with the help of supporting companies like Amazon, Stripe, Etsy, and eBay.

The lockdowns also accelerated some trends in how services are delivered.

For example, Practo, a telehealth company offering online consultations from over 25,000 verified doctors, grew 10x between April and November 2020.

It also makes sense to pay attention to automation and Artificial Intelligence. Although this could decrease many low and middle skill jobs, it also presents an opportunity to harness these new technologies.

For example, a company called Conversion.ai offers Artificial Intelligence assisted writing tools for copywriters, advertisers, and marketers. One freelancer deploying AI may soon be able to do the work of an entire office.

The main point is to understand what is going on in the jobs market, and adjust accordingly.

If you’re a person with skills, you have options and leverage. And it never hurts to continue adding to your skill sets, and exploring new technology that can help you accomplish your goals.

If you’re an employer, it makes sense to review how you hire, how you manage, and what you offer employees. That means understanding what workers value, and putting effort into keeping the best employees who may be tempted to seek greener pastures.
