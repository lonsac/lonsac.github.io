Do Your Values Match the Culture Where You Live?

What happened:

Many remote workers can work from anywhere they want now, and many are choosing to go back to their home towns.

The “boomerang effect” brings people — and their now-remote jobs — back to where they grew up, and where many family and friends still live.

Part of the dynamic driving this trend of moving back home is the benefit of having parents as support structure for child care. This was especially pronounced throughout 2020 as schools and daycares closed (or went crazy with Covid protocols).

People who left smaller cities and towns to go work in big industries in Silicon Valley or New York City are now spreading their expertise and money across the country as they file out of the big tech and business hubs.

What this means:

For a long time, exactly the opposite was true — small towns and mid-size cities were losing out to big cities.

This reorganization will present new opportunities across the country.

For example, many of these technology sector executives with experience at the biggest companies will be big fish in small ponds.

They could continue working for the big shots. But many could opt to start something new and create ground floor opportunities in mid-size cities that were previously only available in a few big cities.

Then there are opportunities to create in-person businesses catering to the boomerang remote workers accustomed to the comforts of the big cities— from restaurants, to services, to entertainment and networking.

Last week we talked about the crime surge in most major cities in the US.

But it’s not just about deciding where NOT to live. It’s smart to make a deliberate decision about where you DO want to be.

What you can do about it:

You can fairly accurately predict what the politics and culture of each region of the USA will look like based on the original colonial immigrants to the area.

According to the book American Nations: A History of the Eleven Rival Regional Cultures of North America, by Colin Woodard, the people who move into a region — or their children — typically end up assimilating to the culture, rather than bringing their own.

(There are notable exceptions; Washington DC has largely displaced the former culture of Maryland and northern Virginia.)

What Woodard calls the “Far West” (Idaho, Montana, Wyoming, North Dakota, and South Dakota) is the most “libertarian” area of the country. That means it tends to favor the free market, little regulation, and reject intervention from Washington DC.

Case in point is Ammon Bundy’s traction in running for governor of Idaho.

You’ll recall Bundy for participating in the 2014 stand-off with federal authorities over his father Cliven Bundy’s, grazing rights on federal land in Nevada. Or Ammon’s 2016 takeover and 40 day occupation of a federal wildlife refuge building in Oregon.

Central to his platform is getting the federal government out of Idaho. The federal government owns 62% of land in Idaho, which is typical of far-west states.

For example the federal government owns 45% of California and 80% of Nevada, compared to only 13% of Florida, 2% of Texas, and .8% of New York, according to the Congressional Research Service.

Bundy wants the state of Idaho to seize federal lands, and open them up to agriculture and development, amid a surge in new movers to Idaho causing a housing shortage.

He says, "When you lose control of the land, you lose control of everything... History and human nature demonstrate that if we build up and create dense and congested cities with large populations, traffic and pollution, we will lose our conservative, traditional values."

Right now states seizing federal land might seem unlikely to occur— but its worth remembering that Bundy has twice been charged with armed opposition to the federal government, and twice been acquitted. His family won the stand-off in Nevada, and continues to graze its cattle on the federal land.

Here, we’re not making a judgment call on the proper political beliefs of tactics. The point is that you can likely find an area of the USA which aligns with your values.

If you think Washington DC is the biggest threat to your future freedom and livelihood, the Far West might not be a bad area of the country to consider.

According to the American Values Atlas, based on 50,000 yearly phone interviews, 50% of residents from Wyoming, 46% from Idaho, and 45% from North Dakota identify as politically conservative.

But again, you have to make the call on what is most important to you. Maybe you think that the attitude in the Far West is a recipe for disaster, inviting punishment from the federal government.

If you would rather live among the highest population of college graduates, the Northeast is a better option.

South Dakota, North Dakota, Utah, Vermont, and New Hampshire have the highest percentage of residents employed in full time jobs. Delaware has the highest portion of retirees of any state.

South Dakota has the highest percentage of homeowners at 78%, followed by a four way tie between Idaho, Iowa, New Hampshire, and West Virginia (74%). New York, California, and New Jersey have the highest percentage of renters.

There will always be trade-offs, which is why there is no easy answer for the definitive “most free” states or countries.

Think about your values, and what you consider to be the biggest threats to your freedom and prosperity. Then use the information you have available to make a deliberate decision about the environment in which you want to live, work, and raise a family.
