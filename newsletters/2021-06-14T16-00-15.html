The Wisdom of Imitation

I once met a painting coach who tells students to copy their favorite artists.

At first, students resist.

In response, the coach tells them to listen for friction. “Do you hear that resistance? It’s the whisper of your unique style.”

Through imitation, we discover our voice.

Learning on the Internet

When it comes to learning, the Internet is the best tool ever invented. Though all of us walk around with the Library of Alexandria in our pockets, it has two big shortcomings:

  1. Depth: It’s over-indexed on introductory content.
    ​
  2. Inconsistency: A surprising amount of popular content isn’t very engaging and is geared for a mass audience of people who don’t want to explore the weeds of an idea.

​
So far, the Internet has adopted a “publish everything online and the algorithms will curate the best stuff” tactic. This tactic is a good start, but we can do better.

By focusing on specific verticals, individuals can curate top content and prevent anything that’s not exceptional from being published in the first place. So far, my favorite initiative is Colossus which aims to become a destination for the world’s top investing and business knowledge.

For entrepreneurs, there’s an opportunity to spin off topic-focused learning platforms where people can explore ideas in-depth. Once you have an audience of the most obsessed people in the world, you can build all kinds of Audience-First Products.

Immersion Learning

I spent most of my childhood struggling to learn Spanish at school. Then, I went to Spain, spoke nothing but Spanish for 30 days, and came back conversational. People at school looked at me and said: “How did you improve so fast?”

For almost any subject, the classroom will get you started, but mastery can only come from immersion.

Language learning makes this principle most evident because it’s impossible to fake competence. In other subjects, you can get away with taxi driver competence just by knowing some key terms.

​Write of Passage is designed around this principle of immersion. The writing begins on day one. The program is designed to teach people enough principles to get started. But students create their own momentum by surrounding themselves with peers and pressing publish throughout the cohort.

Pixar’s Guide to Storytelling

This week, I found a collection of 22 ideas that comprise Pixar’s philosophy of storytelling. This is my favorite one: "Putting it on paper lets you start fixing it. If it stays in your head, a perfect idea, you’ll never share it with anyone."

Writing is like a statue. Where sculptors start with big blocks of marble, writers have to create theirs. That’s what the first draft is all about. It’s a chance to dump ideas onto the page that you can eventually shape in the editing process. Expect these initial ideas to be confusing and filled with unnecessary words.

That’s okay. Nobody’s going to see this draft. The more ideas you put onto the page, the more marble you have to shape into a statue once you start editing. Once it begins, you become like Michelangelo, who is famous for the statue of David in Florence.

Legend has it that he was once asked: "How in God’s name could you have achieved a masterpiece like this from a crude slab of marble?”

To which Michelangelo replied: “All I did was chip away everything that didn’t look like David.”
