The Golden Dilemma
NBER

Gold as an investable asset is widely misunderstood. It tends to be either overly demonized or overly praised depending on who you talk to.

The 2013 paper The Golden Dilemma argues that gold has been a poor hedge of inflation in the short run (years) though it has been a good hedge of inflation in the long run (decades or centuries).

From 1975 through 2012, gold only loosely followed CPI inflation.
The trailing ten-year real gold return was negative from 1988-2005 while inflation was fairly steady over that period.. For the practical investor, 17 years is an awfully long time to wait for gold to kick in.

Over that same time period, there is no correlation between gold and unexpected inflation with the exception of 1980.
However, when viewed over much longer time frames, the stability of gold relative to real purchasing powers is pretty crazy.

In the era of Emperor Augustus (reigned from 27 B.C. to 14 A.D.), a Roman centurion was paid about 38.58 ounces of gold a year (3,750 denarii).

Denominated in ounces of gold, the pay of a U.S. army captain in 1992 is about 28 ounces of gold.

Converted to U.S. dollars, the pay of a Roman centurion was only about 30% greater than the pay of a captain in the U.S. Army circa 2000.
That’s kind of an argument for and against gold. It is something of an argument against gold as something that can achieve "equity-like" returns. Starting 2000 years ago in the year 12 A.D. one dollar compounding at just 1% a year, turns into $439 million over 2,000 years.

However, it is something of an argument in favor of gold as a reliable store of value. The equity value of all companies founded prior to 14 A.D. today is zero.

Gold seems more suited for the once every few hundred years that you have a major crisis, not for the the more typical recessions that have happened every five to ten years or so for the last century.

Beyond its long-term store of value appeal, there is another reason one might consider gold in a diversified portfolio: Gold has low correlations with other tradable assets.
Looking at the five-year rolling betas for gold against a variety of tradable equity and bond market factors, the beta is pretty close to zero.

That means that gold has historically been uncorrelated with equity markets and factors such as value and momentum.

So, the argument for gold in a portfolio would be that though it has a long-run return of near-zero, but, because of its uncorrelated nature to many traditional assets, you can harvest some rebalancing premium while still holding it for the rare, but possible periods of hyperinflation.

Zach Carter on the Real Story of Weimar Hyperinflation
Odd Lots

On the topic of gold, one must o course consider hyperinflation. It is almost cliche now to talk about Weimar hyperinflation of the early 1920s and how that is a lesson that a seemingly developed and robust economy can go through a period of hyperinflation.

As is often the case, the people who like to use this example rarely have a great understanding of what was actually going on in Weimar Germany at the time that hyperinflation set in.

The catalyst for hyperinflation was not merely flagrant money printing by the German central bank, but an invasion by France into West Germany that collapsed confidence and caused a collapse in investor confidence.

I think the important takeaway here is that inflation was high but tolerable until France invaded and then there was a phase shift that triggered run-away inflation. That’s how it happens - slowly then it can take off.

Also important to keep in mind is just how terrible Germany’s situation was in the preceding years: they had just lost the largest war in history, they owed 3-5x their pre-war GDP in reparations payments and their industrial base for growing GDP was completely devastated.
