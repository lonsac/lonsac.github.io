Editing Your Writing

The fresher your eyes, the better you can edit your own writing.

I do that by changing devices. I write on my computer, edit on my iPad, and always make final edits on my phone in bed.

The general idea is that you want your ideas to feel fresh and unfamiliar. Only then can you read them with the critical eye of an outsider. Given that, I also try to step away from my pieces for an extended period of time before I edit them. But if I need to make edits right after I’ve written something, I change the font type, color, and size.

But if there’s any strategy I recommend, it’s to vary the length of your sentences. The screenshot below from Gary Provost explains this idea better than I ever could.

The Three Stages of Entrepreneurship

1) Hustle: You do all the hard work yourself.

2) Management: Hire people to do the things you used to do.

3) Operationalizing: Build systems and codify your decision-making principles so the company can run without your involvement.

Yes, hard work can be good. But there's a certain kind of laziness that eventually drives great entrepreneurs. Once a standard operating procedure has been created, they can offload the task. In contrast, those who insist on doing all the work themselves don't systematize their companies.

Speaking about the founder of a multi-billion dollar Silicon Valley startup, I once asked a friend why the CEO has accomplished so much. He said: “Believe it or not, he’s surprisingly lazy."

Ideas Are Like Fashion

My favorite part of How Philosophers Think is the section about ideological fashions.

Since ideas are invisible, people underestimate the extent to which they can go in and out of fashion. But ideas are like clothing. They change with the times and reveal how much the actions of others influence our decision-making. And it’s not just the conformists. Even counter-cultural styles have consistent tropes. This is how culture acts as an operating system for how we perceive the world. It’s the standard we revert to when we don’t think for ourselves.

As my friend Visakan shows so clearly, we laugh at all the things people used to wear, but if we could see our minds in photos, we’d laugh at all our moral, ideological and intellectual fashions.

Hurting

There’s a scene in Lawrence of Arabia where one man puts out a match with his fingers and doesn’t flinch.

Photo of the Week

I just started an “11 Year Bookclub” with friends where we are going to read the entire Story of Civilization. It’s an 11-volume, 4-million word, 10,000-page series. We plan to read one book per year at a rate of ~75 pages per month.

I like this anecdote from the beginning of the first book: cultures start with custom and with the invention of writing, move towards the law.

Though natural societies are comparatively free from the law, they are ruled by rigid customs. Durant calls them the "bedrock of time-hallowed modes of thought and action which provides a society with some measure of steadiness and order through all absence, changes, and interruptions of law.” Just as habits offer stability to the individual, customs offer stability to a society.

With writing comes the development of laws.

No longer are the laws of the land passed on between the minds of generations. The invention of writing makes that transition possible, as shown by authoritative documents like Hammurabi’s Code and the Magna Carta, both of which were etched in writing.

Custom and law are slightly different, though. Where customs are bottom-up, laws are top-down. And where customs come from the people, laws come from authority figures.
