Principle of Multiple Use

Sharing a team with Tiago Forte is one of the best parts of my job. As much as anything else, it makes me say: "Wow, I'm so lucky to do this.“

During our team retreat last week, we defined our principles of working, which are inspired by his Building a Second Brain course. The stronger our values, the faster we can move and onboard employees. Instead of trying to appeal to everybody, we want to strongly appeal to the small percentage of people we want to attract.

Time and again, our team pitches us on investments such as agencies to hire and software to purchase. We created this chart to clarify how we qualify their proposals. More than anything, we want our investments to benefit multiple parts of the company at the same time.

Our ability to make an impact is downstream of our ability to create leverage. Though every project starts off as low leverage, we can make them high leverage over time.

Specifically, there are two things to observe here: (1) cost is intentionally at the bottom, and (2) we want projects to move from left to right over time.

Write it Up

Human memory is fallible and attention is distractible. Instead of trying to "keep that in mind," we want everybody to capture important information. Our best knowledge should live on paper instead of in the minds of single individuals. That way, we can share and preserve our best thinking.

"Writing it up" happens in two ways: memos and standard operating procedures.

  1. Memos: We preserve our best thinking in 2,000 - 6,000 word memos. The process is inspired by Amazon. Every major company demands a memo because they force clear thinking for the person who owns the project and serve as a central source of truth for the company.
    ​
  2. Standard Operating Procedures: As you'll see in my Personal Assistant video, we create SOPs for every repeatable task. By spending time making them excellent at the beginning, we can consistently refer people to the SOPs page instead of having to individually train every person who wants to do something. Lessons from Leonardo

Tiago turned me onto Walter Isaacson's biography of Leonardo Da Vinci. So far, I've been surprised by two things:

  1. Da Vinci's Commercial Influences: People criticize the Creator Economy for being so commercial, but beginning at the age of 14, Da Vinci apprenticed under Andrea del Verrochio, who ran one of the best workshops in Florence. He was driven as much by economics as creativity. Isaacson writes that the goal of the shop was to "produce a constant flow of marketable art and artifacts rather than nurture creative geniuses yearning to find outsets for their originality." ​
    ​
  2. The Conditions for Creativity: Da Vinci was one of many creatives of his time who were born out of wedlock. The others included Petrarch, Boccaccio, Lorenzo Ghiberti, Filippo Lippi, his son Filippino, and Leon Battista Alberti. Isaacson argues that their creativity was spurred by an ambiguity of status. When you're born out of wedlock, you're simultaneously an insider and an outsider in your own family. That confusion, Isaacson argues, inspires creativity.

​How Tommy Cooper Wrote Jokes​

I love finding stories about creatives who put tons of effort into systems that make their art look effortless. This week, I learned about Tommy Cooper. He was known for magic routines that seemed impulsive.

After he died, people looked through his notebooks and discovered that he was anything but impulsive. He pulled from an encyclopedia of jokes he'd written and filed them under topics like "bullfighting" and "remarks overheard on airplanes."

One draft of his table plans showed exactly where he'd place props too. Time and again, he'd draw where they should be laid out and replicate the setup during his actual performance. This line sticks out: "In the drawings, you can see Cooper thinking carefully about which props should go where. Famously he would 'accidentally' knock things over, but it was all carefully planned."

To him, the art of comedy lay in being so well prepared that he didn't look prepared at all.

Cooper isn't the only one who carefully planned his jokes. Seinfeld brought the same intensity to his craft too.​
​

​
Creative Preparation

While we're on the "things that look easy, but actually require tons of work" theme, let's talk about Neil deGrasse Tyson. When I interviewed him, he said he's already written ~90% of what he says in public.

Like Cooper, he looks like he's speaking off-the-cuff because he's so prepared. Before his first interview with Jon Stewart, he studied how long he'd have to answer questions before being interrupted for comedic value. In order to present complete thoughts without being interrupted, he kept that time limit whenever he went on the show.

Tyson also studied how many days constituted "current events." Two days? Five days? Since it was three days, he meticulously studied the news stories that were likely to be mentioned.

Maybe that's why he's been invited back 14 times.
