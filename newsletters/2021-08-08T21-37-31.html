The US Business Cycle

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than cherry pick good or bad data points to fit a narrative. I don’t normally do this for two premium reports in a row, but we had a lot of new data come in.

The economy is at a high peak (the year-ago period was Q2 of last year, especially for the quarterly numbers like GDP) but is showing initial signs of entering a soft patch in rate-of-change terms, largely due to harder base effects, fiscal stimulus wearing off, and the delta variant.

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

QE Taper Preparation: The Fed Standing Repo Facility

At the end of July, the Fed announced two new standing repo facilities. These facilities let certain entities, domestic and foreign, loan Treasuries to the Fed in exchange for dollars:

The Federal Open Market Committee on Wednesday announced the establishment of two standing repurchase agreement (repo) facilities—a domestic standing repo facility (SRF) and a repo facility for foreign and international monetary authorities (FIMA repo facility). These facilities will serve as backstops in money markets to support the effective implementation of monetary policy and smooth market functioning.

Under the SRF, the Federal Reserve will conduct daily overnight repo operations against Treasury securities, agency debt securities, and agency mortgage-backed securities, with a maximum operation size of $500 billion. The minimum bid rate for repos under the facility will be set initially at 25 basis points, somewhat above the general level of overnight interest rates. Counterparties for this facility will include primary dealers and will be expanded over time to include additional depository institutions.

Under the FIMA repo facility, the Federal Reserve will enter into overnight repurchase agreements as needed with foreign official institutions against their holdings of Treasury securities maintained in custody at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York. The rate for this facility will be set initially at 25 basis points with a per counterparty limit of $60 billion. By creating a temporary source of dollar liquidity for FIMA account holders, the facility can help address pressures in global dollar funding markets that could otherwise affect financial market conditions in the United States.

This is interesting, because right now there is zero demand for repo activities from the Fed. To the contrary, there is nearly a trillion dollars in demand for the Fed’s reverse repo operations. So why implement this?

It seems pretty clear that the Fed is preparing for a gradual taper in its rate of asset purchases. However, it’s unclear if there is enough private sector and foreign sector demand for Treasuries, at the rate of Treasury issuance that the US government plans over the next few years. Plus, the Fed doesn’t want a repeat of what happened in March 2020, where there was a global dollar shortage, the dollar spiked in value, and foreign entities had to sell Treasuries to get dollars. This resulted in the US Treasury market going illiquid with spiking yields, to which the Fed had to respond by buying $1 trillion worth of Treasuries in three weeks, with new money.

So, this set of new repo facilities is interesting, and a number of Fed officials have been talking about implementing them for a couple years. They basically make permanent the types of operations that the Fed did from late 2019 through early 2020. Going forward, certain domestic and foreign holders of Treasuries have assurances that, any time they need to, they can use their Treasuries as collateral with the Fed to get dollars. This increases the fungibility/liquidity of Treasuries with dollars.

At the end of this month, at the Economic Policy Symposium in Jackson Hole, there’s a good chance that the Fed will announce a plan to gradually taper their monthly asset purchases from the current rate of $120 billion. This will mean that the private sector and foreign sector need to absorb more of the US government’s Treasury issuance, and the standing repo facility is ready in the years ahead if those entities find themselves stuffed full of Treasuries and needing dollars.

The combination of an ongoing collateral shortage, improved fungibility between Treasuries and dollars, and notable inflation, should keep negative real yields for Treasuries in place for a while, which is a pretty smart way of doing informal yield curve control without formally pegging long-duration yields.

Infrastructure Bill

The US Senate is coming close to passing a $1 trillion bipartisan infrastructure bill.

This includes about a trillion dollars for roads, bridges, rail, ports, water systems, broadband, energy, and more. It’s spread over many years, so it’s more of a structural bill rather than a macro catalyst here in 2021.

It does not include several additional elements proposed by the Biden Administration, including an allocation towards caregiving of the aged and disabled, workforce development funding, Veterans’ affairs hospital modernization, and a corporate tax raise to pay for some of that. Basically, the Democrats’ plan included the concept of social infrastructure, while the bipartisan plan keeps it more limited to physical infrastructure. These other parts could be revisited in 2022 or 2023, and the odds for those things being passed depend on 2022 midterm election results as well as potential economic slowdowns in the coming years.

One of the things that hung up the 2,700 page $1 trillion infrastructure bill is a few paragraphs about digital asset tax regulation, of all things. In order to raise some revenue, the infrastructure bill would make brokers report gains for clients via a tax form, and report transactions of over $10,000. The goal is to improve tax enforcement on digital asset capital gains. Most of this is already happening at registered exchanges, but for example there are pockets like in DeFi where not a lot of people are paying taxes properly.

The problem, however, is how the bill defined “broker”:

any person who (for consideration) is responsible for regularly providing any service effectuating transfers of digital assets on behalf of another person

This could conceivably include proof-of-work miners, proof-of-stake validators, Lightning node operators, hardware wallet makers, software developers, and all sorts of things that are otherwise not “brokers”. Normal everyday people running some Lightning nodes or running a proof-of-stake validator on Ethereum 2.0 when it is launched, could be brokers under that definition.

Basically, the inherent way that blockchains work, is that they are decentralized and multiple participants are involved in processing blocks of transactions, and they don’t know who to file the tax form on behalf of. It’s impossible.

It’s kind of like policymakers who haven’t used the internet yet trying to pass a bill in the mid-1990s for this “new internet technology”, and saying that hardware routers have to file tax forms “every time they route data packets that include an online payment” and that they have to include personal payment information for those counterparties in their tax filing. The builders and operators of the internet’s routers would naturally say, “Uh, we have no way to do that since they’re encrypted and we don’t know the payer/vendors involved. We’re happy to pay taxes and already do, but this particular method is unenforceable.”

The digital asset lobbyists pushed back on the poorly-described section in the bill, and senators were caught blindsided by this since they thought they would just slip it in but didn’t really know how to word it and didn’t expect this level of pushback. The digital asset industry has a market cap of over $1 trillion so they have some serious lobby firepower.

From there, there have been three different amendments to try to fix the language of the bill, and interestingly, the first two were bipartisan. This wasn’t a Democrat vs Republican issue, which is rare these days. The first bipartisan amendment explicitly clarified that they don’t mean miners/validators, hardware wallets, or software developers. The second competing bipartisan amendment, which was subsequently backed by the White House and Treasury Secretary Yellen, limited the exemptions to proof-of-work mining and hardware wallets (good for that group), but not software developers or proof-of-stake validators (bad for that group). So, that second one is fine for Bitcoin (except possibly for Lightning node operators and certain developers), and bad for Ethereum as a whole and the rest of DeFi.

Many in the industry argued that this second amendment is even worse, since they’re basically picking technology winners and losers now, and that it still doesn’t make sense and is hard to comply with. The concern among industry groups and some senators was that this hastily-written part of a big infrastructure bill would end up pushing innovation overseas, resulting in less tax enforcement rather than more, and the US partaking in a smaller share of growth from the industry than they otherwise would. US-based crypto companies in aggregate are on track to produce $10 billion in revenue this year, and US holders of digital assets collectively have hundreds of billions of dollars of taxable assets in this ecosystem.

Senator Ted Cruz proposed a third amendment that would remove this whole crypto tax enforcement piece from the bill and revisit this separately in the future, saying that this part of the must-pass bill is unworkable and threatens innovation.

As I finish this report on Sunday evening, it still hasn’t passed yet. They have discussed removing the amendments altogether. However, there are formal remarks by the Senators involved that the bill as written only applies to actual brokers. As the executive director of the major DC-based crypto think tank recently described:

However, it does remain a risk in the future that will be monitored by folks in the industry. We’ll see how the final language of the bill develops.

I was asked a lot in the second half of last year and early this year if I thought bitcoin was responsible for the weak price action in gold, and my general answer was “maybe around the margins, but overall gold is still following real rates as it should”. That doesn’t mean gold and bitcoin move inversely, or that many people trade between gold and bitcoin a lot. Instead, it simply means that a number of retail buyers and hedge funds, who would have otherwise bought gold as a hedge against fiat debasement, are instead buying some bitcoin. Many of them have explicitly said so; they buy bitcoin and say that if bitcoin didn’t exist, they’d have to buy gold instead.

Bitcoin Note

For a while I’ve been saying that I need to see a breakout over $41k to become tactically bullish on bitcoin again, even though I already remain structurally bullish and long with a multi-year view since April 2020.

Well, bitcoin rose up and touched $42k, dipped to about $38k, made higher highs at $45k, and has since dipped to $43k/$44k. That looks pretty healthy.

Willy Woo, an on-chain bitcoin analyst, has been showing evidence of a supply shock forming in recent weeks. Bitcoin has been gradually moving from liquid “weak hand” addresses to illiquid “strong hand” addresses that often buy and rarely sell:

Chart Source: @woonomic

Here’s the supply/demand situation. Millions of people are dollar-cost averaging and “HODLing” their bitcoins, with little or no intention to sell any time soon. Institutions are gradually allocating as well. The center of bitcoin mining is moving to North America from China, and those new mining companies have better access to capital markets. So rather than selling newly-created bitcoins that they generate in order to pay their expenses, those miners are “HODLing” their bitcoins on their balance sheets indefinitely, rather than selling, and using equity or debt financing to pay their expenses.

This constitutes a supply shock. Supply is limited, and of that limited supply, not many bitcoins are liquid and available.

Meanwhile, the weak hands that bought at the $50-$60k top this spring on the Grayscale neutral arbitrage pump without fully understanding what they held and why, have generally already sold.

This is reviving comparisons among bitcoin bulls to the 2013 double-pump bull run with a six-month correction in the middle.

Chart Source: LookIntoBitcoin

Bitcoin has been broadly de-leveraged, with the Grayscale neutral arbitrage premium phased out, and with a lot of leveraged trades long-since liquidated. Sentiment and valuations have been reset. Meanwhile, people continue to dollar-cost average into it.

That being said, although various on-chain indicators look good here, I can’t know for sure how it will perform in terms of price over any 6-month period. My main thesis on it is more long-term oriented, and even that is simply a matter of probabilities and watching ongoing development and network effects. The bear case is that what bitcoin is doing now, is just a bull trap or dead cat bounce, with a lower-low ahead in this cycle.

Jurrien Timmer, director of global macro at Fidelity, has a good long-term chart on bitcoin that he updates from time to time:

Ethereum Note

Ethereum broke above the $2,900 threshold that I’ve been mentioning, which I’ve said would renew my base case towards a tactical bull scenario:

Significant Bear Tail Risk: SEC Enforcement

The US Securities and Exchange Commission, SEC, is stepping up its enforcement on cryptos. Gary Gensler, chairman of the SEC, gave a big speech about it last week.

Bitcoin itself is classified as a commodity, not a security, and Gensler has praised its technological innovations several times. However, many cryptos in the space are considered to be securities. So far, the SEC has not been very clear about how it classifies digital assets as securities or not securities, although there is pretty clear legal precedent.

The SEC has historically had conflicting views on Ethereum as it pertains to regulation (specifically, whether “ether” or “ETH” is a security or commodity). On one hand, they’ve said that basically any crypto that does an initial coin offering, is a security. Ethereum did an initial coin offering back in 2014, and its creator even compared their initial coin offering to Ripple (which is now being sued by the SEC for selling their own allegedly-unlicensed securities called XRP). On the other hand, the SEC (under the previous chairman, rather than Gensler) has previously said that ETH, like BTC, is not a security.

However, BTC unlike ETH, had no initial coin offering. BTC was released to the public and mined from scratch in public with no pre-mine, unlike ETH. Nobody even knows who the creator of BTC is besides his pseudonym, since he stepped away from the project early on and left it to volunteers to continue. There was never a central foundation that raised capital for the Bitcoin network or operated it.

Unlike Ripple Labs which continued to sell XRP tokens each year, the Ethereum Foundation sold all of its tokens in 2014 in one big chunk over several months, and therefore seems to be well past the legal statute of limitations for certain legal actions. That doesn’t necessarily mean that their token ETH isn’t a security, which could come with various implications if it ends up being the case. Those implications may or may not affect price, since XRP has been able to levitate despite ongoing legal issues from the SEC.

Gary Gensler has been making the media rounds, and he made clear the list of attributes of a security, referencing prior US Supreme Court decisions. Rohan Vora made a funny video last week outlining how directly Ethereum matches the SEC’s definition of a security, as Gensler describes it. Basically, if a group raises money selling a new coin, and there is an anticipation of profits based on the efforts of that group, then it’s a security. The Ethereum Foundation sold coins as a pre-mine in an initial coin offering to raise money, along with promises for development, marketing, branding, and business plans from their team, with publicly-stated anticipation of token price appreciation to benefit the Ethereum Foundation and those who buy in.

So it remains to be seen how the SEC will enforce this over the next year. Will ETH be grandfathered in due to the statute of limitations and sufficient decentralization, or is it a security, which it seems to be? Either way, Gary Gensler made it clear that many coins that trade on centralized exchanges and Ethereum DeFi protocols are securities, even if they decide ETH itself is not. And the SEC could label something to be a security and prevent third parties from selling it, even if the SEC can’t go after the originator of the token pre-sale due to expired statutes. It’s all very complex, subject to interpretation, and I’m not a lawyer.

ETH Summary

Overall, although I don’t view it as money, I view ETH as an interesting speculation, and for all intents and purposes basically a security.

One one hand, it’s a very well-designed bubble in the making, with staking that reduces existing liquid supply at least until Ethereum 2.0 comes out at an unidentified date, combined with the EIP 1559 hard fork to reduce new supply issuance and turn it deflationary. Over the next 6-12 months, this could be very powerful for ETH price appreciation. In a particularly bullish case, as momentum traders hop back onboard, Ethereum could very well outperform Bitcoin until the Ethereum 2.0 unlock. Right now, there is a ton of enthusiasm around digital art hosted on Ethereum.

On the other hand, one day we could randomly wake up and see ETH down 30% overnight because Gary Gensler says ETH is a security and that exchanges can’t sell it. Unlike the Bitcoin ecosystem which is inherently built to withstand state attacks, the Ethereum ecosystem has multiple centralized attack surfaces that regulators can go after in various ways, including the Ethereum Foundation and Consensys (which owns Infura and Metamask; two central parts of the Ethereum ecosystem, and which provides significant development for the main Ethereum chain and layer 2 scaling solutions), and various developers building out different parts of the DeFi ecosystem.

Enterprise Products Partners Analysis

In addition to energy producers, I continue to like high-quality energy transporters, which are less subject to price and more subject to volumes. Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) is my favorite, although investors should be aware that it does issue a K-1 form, since it’s a MLP rather than a corporation. That means investors have to file an extra tax form during tax season.

Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs

The annual distribution yield is 8%, with 23 consecutive years of increases. They have a 1.6x distributable cash flow coverage ratio, and their most recent distribution increase was conservative at only 1.1% year-over-year as they deal with this uncertain time. They also do some opportunistic unit buybacks if they have spare capital after reinvestment and distributions.

They continue to trade at below-average valuations, with a distribution yield higher than its historical average, and a price-to-cashflow ratio that is below its historical average.

Their business is highly-diversified, as as they transport crude oil, natural gas, natural gas liquids, refined products, and petrochemicals. The majority of these products are used for purposes other than vehicle fuel, and thus are not easily displaced even with the growth trend of electric vehicles. They increasingly focus on exporting to various parts of Asia, where demand continues to grow strongly.

Chart Source: EPD May 2021 Investor Deck

EPD recently reported that its volumes are rebounding from the pandemic lows, and that its ongoing growth projects are on-time and on-budget. Management has expressed on their most recent conference call that they think that supply shortages, combined with gradually growing demand, are likely to result in higher energy prices. And they’re looking out several years as they forecast these trends, not quarter by quarter.

They’ve also discussed long-term opportunities for repositioning their pipeline system expertise for carbon sequestration and/or hydrogen fuel transportation over time, but say that they still need more clarity on policy and the energy mix going forward.

In the meantime, they issue debt with a 4.5% average yield and a 20-year average duration, and use it to build assets that typically generate 12% returns on invested capital, while returning large amounts of cash to unitholders each year.

They maintain the highest credit rating in the midstream industry, tied with a few other blue chips in the industry, with low debt metrics relative to most peers. Their debt is fixed-rate, while their income streams on their various assets can generally go up with inflation. They no longer need to issue equity to fund growth, and if during any particular year growth opportunities are lackluster, they can instead use excess cash after distributions to buy back some of their units.

Overall, I’m still viewing the second half of this year through a somewhat inflationary lens. The economy will inevitably weaken in rate-of-change terms due to base effects and stimulus wearing off, but inflation could very well be rather persistent. Consensus expectations among economists seem to be tilted towards disinflation, but rent inflation and wage inflation remain significant, even if things like lumber prices and used car prices have rolled over.

I like to have exposure to inflationary hedges in this environment, such as energy stocks, while also maintaining positions in some rather defensive assets like TIPS (STIP), gold, and a diverse basket of healthcare stocks (BMY, CVS, CI, etc). Some of the tech platforms continue to look attractive, like Facebook (FB), but it pays to be cautious about valuations in general.
