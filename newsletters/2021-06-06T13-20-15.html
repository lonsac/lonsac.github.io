Quebec wants nation status recognized— with its own flag emoji

What happened:

A nation and a country are not always the same thing.

Quebec is a province in the country of Canada. But Quebec speaks a different language—French— and has its own distinct culture and history.

This makes it a nation within a country.

That doesn’t necessarily mean Quebec wants total independence from Canada. But it wouldn’t mind more autonomy over its own affairs, and more recognition of its unique nation status among the Canadian provinces.

The latest effort by the Quebec legislature involved passing a unanimous motion to create an emoji of the Quebec flag as soon as possible.

Emojis are the little icons and smiley faces which can accompany text messages and emails. And, as the legislature notes, other nations with some level autonomy from the larger government, such as Catalonia and Scotland, already have their own flag emoji.

The motion recognizes “the importance for Quebec to have its distinct visual identity in every facet of cyberspace,” and therefore “file an application with the Unicode Consortium, the organization responsible for managing emojis on the major global platforms, to add the Québec flag to the emoji bank as soon as possible.”

This, however, isn’t Quebec’s only, or most serious, effort to be recognized as a nation.

Bill 96 in the Quebec legislature would adjust the Quebec Constitution to say “Quebecers form a nation,” and “French shall be the only official language of Quebec. It is also the common language of the Quebec nation.”

What this means:

Quebec is part of a continuum of regions around the world seeking some level of independence from the larger political body it is part of.

Quebec seems more interested in preserving its cultural and national autonomy, as opposed to gaining total political and governing independence.

In April, we discussed movements for total governing independence in Texas, Catalonia, Scotland, and West Cape, South Africa.

All these movements aim to decentralize power and bring it closer to the local level.

And the strength of autonomous regions could serve as a sort of Plan B to maintain social order if (or when) the irresponsible spending and monetary policies of most centralized governments cause chaos.

For example, Bermuda is a British overseas territory and the citizens have a British passport, but it is self governing apart from military, international relations, and certain courts.

Puerto Rico is another example of an autonomous region. It’s a territory the US, but sets its own tax laws, which is why it can offer amazing tax incentives unavailable to Americans anywhere else.
