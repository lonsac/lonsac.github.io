Monetary Policy Update

In my previous report, I discussed the upcoming Jackson Hole Symposium where central bankers from multiple countries gather annually. The big thing that participants were waiting for is to see if Chairman Powell would say anything clear on the Fed’s plan to taper asset purchases. My thoughts were:

I’ll be looking for comments from Fed Chairman Powell at the Jackson Hole event on August 27, since that has been the consensus day on when he might announce an asset purchase tapering plan. Recent comments by Kaplan and others have made that a bit less likely. All else being equal, a delay in tapering, if it were to occur, should be somewhat dollar bearish and gold bullish.

My base case, loosely held, is that the Fed will discuss conditions for tapering at the Jackson Hole event, rather than announce a specific tapering date. In other words, they might define more specifically what triggers in terms of unemployment and/or inflation would lead them to taper. If it works out that way, this would in some sense be a tapering delay, citing the delta variant.

-August 22nd report

And that’s pretty much what happened. A number of Fed officials came out with somewhat hawkish statements about starting taper right away and finishing early in 2022, meaning the Fed’s balance sheet would be flat from there. But Powell, in his key speech, struck a dovish tone. He emphasized that broad measures of unemployment are still high, labor participation is still low, that there remains uncertainty with the virus, etc. He talked about the appropriateness of eventually reducing asset purchases, maybe even this year, but gave no clear timeline. Basically, markets got the good-cop-bad-cop routine, with Powell playing the good cop.

The speech was well-written but strategically said very little. Basically, it explained the well-known risk of tightening monetary policy against transitory inflation effects vs the risk of being too slow and letting persistent inflation run hot.

He did make a couple things clear from his perspective. First, the Fed still isn’t committed to whether they will begin tapering their monthly rate of asset purchases within this year or not, and are looking at incoming data. They’re still emphasizing inflation as transitory, and labor conditions are improving but still unsatisfactory. Second, even when they do taper their rate of asset purchases, they will not raise interest rates until, to use his words, maximum employment is achieved.

Why The Jobs Disconnect?

A common narrative for a while has been that because the federal government is paying enhanced unemployment benefits, it entices people not to work. Hence, labor participation remains low, despite job openings being at record highs, because people are paid to stay home. Sounds right.

However, many US states did cease providing unemployment benefits in May 2021, which cut off the federal aid in those states as well. As the WSJ recently reported, there was minimal difference in the rate of job creation from that point over the subsequent few months between states that ended the benefits and those that kept them:

States that ended enhanced federal unemployment benefits early have so far seen about the same job growth as states that continued offering the pandemic-related extra aid, according to a Wall Street Journal analysis and economists.

Several rounds of federal pandemic aid boosted the amount of unemployment payments, most recently by $300 a week, and extended them for as long as 18 months. The extra benefits are set to expire nationwide next week. But 25 states ended the financial enhancement over the summer, and most of them also moved to end other pandemic-specific unemployment programs such as benefits for gig and self-employed workers.

Nonfarm payrolls rose 1.33% in July from April in the 25 states that ended the benefits and 1.37% in the other 25 states and the District of Columbia, the Journal analysis of Labor Department data showed.

Now that the enhanced benefits all expired nationwide anyway as of this weekend, why should we care about this difference (or lack thereof) between states? Well, mainly because it shows that the labor shortage likely won’t magically fix itself overnight simply because the enhanced unemployment benefits go away, as half of the states have already shown for months now.

Instead, I think the labor shortage is more structural than that. Wages haven’t kept up with housing prices, healthcare, childcare, and other capital goods and services for a while. Hedonic adjustments and cheaper electronics/attire out of Asia that keep official CPI low, don’t make up for the fact that the essentials in life have gone up a lot. Now used car prices have increased a lot too.

Overall, I think the 2020s are shaping up to be a decade of higher average wage growth than the 2010s. Globalization (measured as global trade as a percentage of global GDP) has likely peaked for a while, wages haven’t kept up with the real cost of living, and it’ll take some financial enticement to get people back to full work levels and fill in the labor gaps.

That bodes bullish for inflation remaining above the Fed’s target level for longer than some analysts seem to expect, although we could get periodic dips along the way.

Cyclical Stagflation is Here

Economic reports have been surprising consensus economists to the downside, while inflation has been surprising them to the upside:

Source: Callum Thomas

Stagflation is a relatively rare situation (for developed markets) where economic growth is low but inflation is high.

The worst type of stagflation is when the GDP is actually contracting into a recession while inflation is running hot. A milder version of stagflation is when economic growth is positive but decelerating, while inflation is hot.

The second situation (mild stagflation) is happening now in the US. Most indicators of economic growth are slowing down, and some indicators like retail sales are in actual contraction, while inflation remains hot. It remains unclear how long this period will persist but that’s the current data.

The Federal Reserve has two mandates: maximize employment and maintain stable prices. Usually during recessions when unemployment goes up, inflation goes down due to declining demand for things, so the Fed becomes more simulative to try to improve the employment situation. And usually during economic booms when unemployment goes down, inflation heats up a bit due to rising demand for things, so the Fed tightens monetary policy to try to tame it.

But stagflation is tough, because employment isn’t maximized while inflation is running hot anyway. What does the Fed do in that situation? Try to boost unemployment, or try to tame inflation? It’s hard to do both at the same time with the tools they have.

So far, they’ve made it clear via their statements over the past year that they will pick employment over price stability when push comes to shove. And this is to be expected, given where we are in the long-term debt cycle.

In the late 1970s, faced with stagflationary conditions (but low debt levels relative to GDP), Fed Chairman Paul Volcker chose to prioritize inflation reduction even if it meant putting the economy into a recession and spiking unemployment levels. In the 2020s, faced with stagflationary conditions (and high debt levels relative to GDP), Powell is kind of forced into the other direction; prioritizing employment even if it means letting inflation run hot.

Basically, it seems that retail speculators have been largely washed out, while bigger players are the ones back in, focusing on bitcoin and smart contract platforms and five or six or seven figure NFTs.

In other words, it’s a more specific type of euphoria focused on smart contracts and NFTs among larger investors, rather than broad-based euphoria across the board. This is good; it suggests that bitcoin likely still has more room to run in this cycle, rather than the whole space looking extremely toppy like it did back in May 2021. There is an indication that participants in the market are separating and pricing coins based on underlying application potential, rather than buying across the board.

I think Solana (currently the 7th largest crypto by market cap) has a decent shot of catching up with Cardano (currently the 3rd largest) in terms of market capitalization, although I don’t invest in either of them. The main draw for Solana right now is that they are building out semi-decentralized exchanges and other applications, and their fees are much lower than Ethereum.

Bitcoin bulls often criticize Ethereum for being insufficiently decentralized. Ethereum bulls lately have been criticizing Solana for being insufficiently decentralized. And that in my view is correct on both counts; each blockchain makes certain trade-offs, and usually some of the decentralization is sacrificed in favor of additional functionality and efficiency. The problem is that the primary application of a blockchain is to make something decentralized. That can cause a slippery slope towards greater and greater centralization like we see with Solana currently. Solana has semi-decentralized projects running on top of a very centralized base layer.

Ethereum offered more application functionality on its base layer than Bitcoin, but at a cost of more complexity and higher node requirements, along with difficulty bombs that keep control more in the hands of Ethereum’s developers, allowing them to change monetary policy and implement hard forks.

Solana, with new technology, has similar functionality and much higher transaction throughput than Ethereum, and thus much lower transaction fees, but this too comes at the cost of far higher node requirements. You need datacenter-level hardware and bandwidth to run a Solana validator. And although Solana is open-source, it has centralized organizations and VC capital behind it, making the leadership and ownership of the coins rather centralized, which is of particular concern for a proof-of-stake protocol. Plus, Solana is still in beta mode and does manual stake slashing by developers; meaning their whole proof-of-stake consensus model is still totally centralized at the moment. It’s decentralized in name only, rather than in real terms. And yet its market capitalization is $40 billion, despite still being in beta mode.

That’s why I consider bitcoin to be a form of digital money, while I view most other coins to be varying shades of digital equities, with centralized development hubs, technical roadmaps, marketing budgets, and in some cases outright manual blockchain overrides by developers. Some have this problem more than others. This is why I primarily hold bitcoins as my digital asset, and only do some experimentation for research purposes with ether, DeFi, etc to make sure I stay on top of how the space is evolving.

Do users care about Solana’s high level of centralization? So far, no. With Ethereum’s fees skyrocketing, making it unsuitable for most small users, Solana is a cheaper DeFi alternative. Solana’s total-value locked or “TVL” in DeFi projects is up about 180% over the past month to $4.2 billion. Ethereum’s TVL is only up about 15% during that timeframe, although from a much larger base. Solana is taking a small but growing market share of the smart contract space, and it remains to be seen if that will taper off or continue.

Uranium Fundamental Review

Before getting to what Sprott is doing, I’ll do a quick review of the uranium market and my reason for becoming bullish last year. I recommend reading the full October 2020 report I published, but we can do a quick review here with new charts.

Many decades ago, uranium was mined for nuclear weapons, but by the 1960s, it became more about civilian nuclear energy. The price had a huge bull run in the 1970s, known as the “Golden Age of Nuclear”, along with most other commodities. It then went through a multi-decade bear market, and then had another huge bull market in the 2000s along with many other commodities again, as China rose up and increased its demand for commodities.

People often think of nuclear energy as a dying industry, and to some extent it is indeed stagnating in the West. However, the world continues to gradually grow its nuclear fleet, as China, India, and countries in the Middle East and Eastern Europe continue to build new reactors. It’s a virtually carbon-free source of baseload power, and a nuclear facility can last 50-100 years.

Meanwhile, many old nuclear bombs were dis-assembled and used as fuel for nuclear reactors. This resulted in a weird market for uranium prices, because a large amount of uranium supply came from these old stockpiles rather than just from new mines.

For a nuclear power plant, the cost of uranium is very low compared to the billions of dollars it takes to build the nuclear reactor facility, and then they maintain financing for that sunk cost for decades. So, even if there is a uranium supply shortage, nuclear utilities are inflexible buyers, basically buying at whatever price is needed to get it in order to keep their multi-billion-dollar facility active. For this reason, uranium buyers often perform multi-year contracts to secure their supply of uranium, and the futures market tends to be illiquid, with low volume and without much price discovery.

The unusual supply situation (mines + secondary stockpiles), along with this demand inflexibility and low futures volume and the occasional disaster that reduces demand, makes uranium particularly extreme in terms of boom/bust cycles.

Currently, nuclear utilities are consuming more uranium than is mined per year, and have been for many years. The difference is made up from tapping into around 50 million pounds of secondary supplies and existing inventories per year:

Chart Source: SPUT Factsheet 

The price that uranium would have to be at in order to bring new mines into production is often estimated to be over $60 USD. Currently, uranium is less than $40 USD (and was around $30 USD back in October 2020). So, uranium production is not covering existing demand, and uranium trades at a price that is below the cost to profitably make more mines for it.

Some large miners like Cameco and Kazatomprom actually buy secondary uranium on the market and re-sell it to their nuclear utility customers within their long-term contract framework. This is because in many cases it’s cheaper to buy it than to mine it and deplete their reserves.

The question then becomes, how much secondary supply is there? How long can this weird situation persist?

There are a range of estimates that suggest that secondary supplies are getting pretty tight. There have been a number of studies on it. Mining.com had a good set of charts on this subject a few years ago, from the Bank of Montreal.

We do know that utility inventories are pretty low, especially in the United States, so they’ll need to buy more uranium in upcoming years:

Chart Source: Yellow Cake August 2021 Presentation

In summary, the fundamental thesis is that uranium for civilian nuclear energy use is still a gradually growing industry on a worldwide basis, and annual demand exceeds annual production, while the price of uranium is below the cost of new mine production. The biggest miners literally buy uranium at market prices and re-sell it to their customers, because it’s more cost-effective than expanding their existing production. Uranium buyers are rather price-insensitive if there is any supply constraint, and we don’t know exactly how much secondary supplies are left. Prices would need to go up considerably in order to bring large new supplies to market.

The Sprott NAV Premium Catalyst

As previously mentioned, Sprott Inc (SII) acquired the Uranium Participation Corporation, which was a Canadian company that simply held uranium. Unlike an ETF, its price can fluctuate relative to its net asset value (the current market value of the uranium it holds), resulting in periods of premiums or discounts to net asset value. When I recommended it in October 2020, it was trading at about a 20% discount to net asset value, meaning we were basically buying each dollar of uranium for eighty cents.

It subsequently rose in price to a mild premium over NAV. So we benefited from the uranium price going up, and the discount for the fund turning into a premium.

Sprott bought the Uranium Participation Corporation in mid-July 2021 and renamed it to the Sprott Physical Uranium Trust or “SPUT” for short. The ticker symbols are U.U and U.UN on the Toronto Stock Exchange, and SRUUF on over-the-counter markets in the US. It’s a closed-end fund that buys and holds uranium.

However, it’s getting a bit more interesting. Sprott doesn’t particularly want the fund to trade at a premium to NAV, since that makes it less attractive for new buyers (who wants to buy a $1 of uranium for $1.10?). So, when it does trade at a premium to NAV, which it is now, Sprott intends to issue new shares to buy more uranium.

On August 17th 2021, Sprott issued a press release saying that they are willing to issue up to $300 million USD in new shares to buy uranium at the market price. They quickly used up over half of that, and even if they quickly use the next half, they can simply do another authorization for more.

Uranium futures have already been in a bull market since late 2016, and Sprott might have just accelerated it:

The uranium market, as previously mentioned, is not very liquid, and for a while there hasn’t been a lot of price discovery going on. Sprott is basically calling the bluff on the market, by saying “you want to sell uranium below the cost of new production? Sure, we’ll soak that supply all up, thanks!” Now with this active buyer, the uranium market seems to be getting some price discovery, and at a time when many US utilities have rather low inventories of it.

For as long as the Sprott Physical Uranium Trust trades at a premium to NAV, they can keep issuing new shares to buy more uranium. And they have an incentive to do so because the larger their fund is, the larger their management fees are. Once they own the uranium, they never have to sell it.

If we zoom out, I still think there is plenty of upside potential ahead in the coming years. I’d consider trimming my uranium assets if the price gets around $60, although we could easily have a supply squeeze that sends it much higher again:

There’s a reasonable possibility of price pullbacks next week or next month, and I think those would represent decent entry points. The price surged up last week, so I wouldn’t be surprised if it takes a rest, but as I look out for the long run, I still like these prices a lot and Sprott is in a position to buy more uranium and continue providing a bullish catalyst.

Risks

I like the uranium investment because it’s rather uncorrelated to most other investments. It simply comes down to keeping the existing global fleet of reactors fueled with uranium, and uranium is currently below the cost of new production. Economic cycles don’t affect the uranium thesis much.

However, there are some key risks unique to the uranium market.

Price Volatility

I started writing this piece on Wednesday, and uranium utterly soared on Thursday and Friday, which was annoying. I would have preferred it to have waited until I got this piece published, but I already do have existing positions in a number of the portfolios since October 2020 anyway. I remain structurally long.

While I am structurally bullish on uranium, we could certainly get some volatile pullbacks along the way. If the market moves too much too fast, it could take a significant breather. If someone doesn’t have any position, then starting a small one can be a good idea. From there, it’s perhaps best to buy dips that occur, rather than chase it.

In other words, this is probably a multi-year story in the making, and it won’t be a straight line up. It’s a key industry to watch for opportunities, rather than a six-week trade idea.

Sprott’s Premium Going Away

Sprott’s uranium trust currently trades at an 11% premium to NAV, after recent excitement. This is both good and bad.

The bad news is that if you buy into it, you’re buying each dollar of uranium for $1.11, which isn’t a great deal compared to our initial purchase price. If the premium goes back to zero, you can lose about 10% of your investment, even without the price of uranium itself changing. Alternatively, you can buy some uranium miners like Cameco, Kazatomprom, some of the juniors, or some of the ETFs like URNM or URA. They don’t have a premium over NAV, but as miners they have all sorts of operational risks, valuation risks, etc.

The good news is that this supply squeeze can keep happening as long as Sprott’s fund has a premium to NAV. They can just keep issuing new shares to buy secondary uranium and pull it off the market, which is great for uranium’s price. There are only a few main vehicles to get exposure to uranium in the world, plus a small handful of decent uranium mining stocks. The whole market is small, so as it gathers interest from larger pools of capital, there’s a lot more money that can come in.

If Sprott’s premium goes away, it may be a catalyst to trim your uranium position for a period of time, especially if there has been a lot of positive price action leading up to that moment. When the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust’s (GBTC) premium went away earlier this year, it stopped them from buying bitcoin, which in my view contributed significantly to the bitcoin and broader crypto price correction that occurred by late spring. We could see a similar situation happen in the uranium market if Sprott’s premium goes away, since they would stop buying uranium.

Nuclear Disaster 

If there is a fourth major nuclear disaster like Three Mile Island, Chernobyl, or Fukushima, that could significantly reduce uranium demand and send price sharply lower. The Fukushima disaster quickly forced all Japanese nuclear reactors offline, and they only started to come back online slowly, after many years and government approvals.

All three disasters, even though they happened in different decades, were from facilities built in the 1960s and 1970s. Together, even at the highest estimates of long-run radiation deaths, they killed fewer people than die from coal pollution each year. Plus, technology has improved a lot since then, and the 2011 Fukushima disaster resulted in a lot more focus on safety among global reactor operators.

However, it remains possible that another meltdown could occur at any of the hundreds of reactors around the world, and this could permanently derail the fundamental thesis. We could wake up and see uranium asset down massively in a day, with a disaster on headline news. The way to manage this tail risk is to maintain a reasonable position size in the industry.

Final Thoughts

For environments with slowing economic growth but with high inflation and sturdy commodity prices (stagflation), there are a couple main themes we want to see in an investment.

One, we want to see companies or assets that can grow even if economic growth is slow. In other words, they have a wide economic moat, and a structural growth pattern that takes market share from other industries. Or, they pay high enough dividends and keep their revenues up with inflation, so that even if they chop around without much real growth, the dividends provide useful income and total returns.

Two, we want to see companies whose revenue is variable, but whose expenses are rather fixed. Inflation puts pressure on the profit margins of companies whose expenses rise as fast as their revenue. We want to see pricing power; companies who can keep up with inflation.

An example of a company that I like is Intercontinental Exchange (ICE), the company that owns the New York Stock Exchange and some other businesses. An exchange has relatively fixed expenses but their revenues are quite variable with futures volumes, trading volumes, asset price inflation, and so forth.

Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs

Royalty companies, exchanges, reasonably-valued technology producers, and undervalued assets broadly, all fall into this category. The Horizon Kinetics Inflation Beneficiaries ETF (INFL) focuses on a companies with those characteristics.

Scarce monetary assets like gold, that naturally don’t have to worry about profit margin compression like equities do, historically also perform well in periods of stagflation. Bitcoin would be another example of an asset in this monetary category, albeit with a shorter track record.
