Stages of Fasting

Anabolic

0-4 hours

1 Food breaks down into glucose, amino acids, and fatty acids.

2 Leptin increases and ghrelin decreases.

3 Pancreas secretes insulin.

4 Insulin shuttles nutrients into cells to be stored as glycogen, fat, muscle, or used as energy.

Summary

Primary fuel source: glucose from your previous meal.

Between 0 and 4 hours, your body is still going through the process of ingesting and storing the last thing you ate. After your body breaks down carbs, protein, and fat into glucose, amino acids, and fatty acids, your body metabolizes them for energy or stores them for later use. Depending on the last thing you ate before starting your fast, a few different hormones could be at play in your bloodstream. ### Deep Dive

If you've eaten a meal heavy in carbohydrates, you'll have a lot of glucose in your bloodstream. That means a rise in insulin. Your pancreas secretes insulin to help shuttle glucose into cells where it can immediately be used for energy production or stored for later use, either as glycogen (the short-term storage of glucose) or as fat (long-term storage). Four hours after your last meal, glucose and insulin have most likely returned to “normal” pre-meal levels.

You'll also experience a fluctuation in ghrelin and leptin. Ghrelin, the “hunger hormone” is what turns on your appetite. Leptin does the opposite. After a meal, your ghrelin levels fall and your leptin levels rise, signaling to your brain that you're full.

The 0-4 hour time frame is what we call an anabolic, or growth period since nutrients are available and the body can burn them, build muscle with them, or store them. It’s essential for long term health to balance cellular growth with cellular breakdown and cleanup. The prevalence of snacking and frequent meals, however, mean most people spend the bulk of our waking hours (and some of our sleeping ones too) in this anabolic state.

Catabolic

4-16 hours

1 Blood glucose continues to drop.

2 Release of insulin is low, but release of glucagon starts to rise.

3 Glucagon triggers liver glycogen breakdown.

4 Suppression of mTOR “opens the door” for autophagy.

Summary

Primary fuel source: glucose from liver glycogen and fatty acids from fat stores.

During the 4-16 hour phase, your body switches to the catabolic, or breakdown, state where stored nutrients including glycogen and fat are put to use. Once blood glucose and insulin levels drop, your pancreas will release glucagon—a catabolic hormone that stimulates the breakdown of glycogen (stored glucose) for energy. Since glucose is still a prominent fuel source in this phase, your metabolism will attempt to break down enough glycogen to keep your blood glucose in the “normal” range (about 70-99 mg/dL).

Deep Dive

Glucose is still your primary, preferred fuel source, but when your glycogen reserves are nearing empty, you'll start using fat stores and ketone bodies to make up the difference. The exact time that your body starts shifting from using glucose to fat and ketones for energy depends on how much glycogen you've got stored away and how much energy you're burning throughout the day. This switch happens faster if you're exercising since you'll burn through your glycogen stores more quickly; it happens slower if you have compromised metabolic health (i.e. insulin resistance).

As your body exits the “anabolic state” you begin to make room for cellular cleanup, a process known as autophagy. This is the process by which cellular debris are disposed of, and worn-down cellular components are recycled. Animal studies have demonstrated the value of autophagy in protecting against age-associated dysfunction in important organs including the brain, heart, liver, kidneys, and pancreas. The process isn’t yet well-studied in humans, but we do know that autophagy is diminished by the presence of growth factors in the anabolic state.

One particular growth regulator, the mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR), has a prominent role in the switching back and forth between an anabolic state and autophagy. The switch activity of mTOR is largely influenced by nutrient intake, particularly protein, but also other nutrient and growth factors including glucose and insulin. AS you enter a catabolic state, fasting diminishes all of these signals. While there is always some level of autophagy taking place in your body, suppression of mTOR opens the door for a much higher rate of cellular cleanup.

Fat Burning

16-24 hours

1 Asglycogen becomes depleted, glucose from gluconeogenesis starts to rise.

2 Lipolysis breaks down fat stores.

3 Fatty acids are mobilized for energy.

4 The liver begins to synthesize ketones from excess energy produced from fatty acid oxidation.

5 AMPK activity promotes autophagy.

Summary

Primary fuel source: fatty acids from fat stores and glucose from gluconeogenesis.

Your body is switching into fat-burning mode now. In this zone, your body still uses glucose (in fact, it will always use some amount of glucose!), but it is now significantly more dependent on fatty acids for fuel. Glycogen is significantly depleted, so your body produces glucose through a process called gluconeogenesis while fat cells release free fatty acids through a process called lipolysis. In the liver, overflow from fat oxidation is transformed into ketone bodies.

Deep Dive

Between 16 and 24 hours, blood glucose levels will be reduced by about 20%. At this point, fatty acids from fat oxidation become your primary fuel source, but your brain still needs glucose to function. With your glycogen stores tapped, your body makes glucose from non-carbohydrate sources like lactate, glycerol, and amino acids through gluconeogenesis. Yes, your body can actually make sugar out of protein. In this zone, your glucose production from gluconeogenesis increases from approximately 50 g/d to around 100 g/d. Most of this glucose is used by the brain while the rest of your body switches over to fat for fuel.

Decreasing levels of glycogen and insulin trigger activity of an energy-sensing protein called adenosine monophosphate kinase (AMPK), which further promotes the activation of autophagy. So you've taken the brakes off of autophagy by reducing mTOR, and now you've started to press the gas pedal. Things really start to speed up from here as the presence of AMPK further inhibits mTOR.

Ketosis

24-72 hours

1 Liver glycogen stops being a source of glucose.

2 Gluconeogenesis reaches its peak and begins to drop off.

3 Blood ketones increase; BHB is usually 0.5-2 mM.

4 Total ghrelin output decreases.

5 Diminished growth signals and increased AMPK activity further promote autophagy.

Summary

Primary fuel source: fatty acids and ketone bodies plus glucose from gluconeogenesis.

You are now entering a multi-day fast. Between 24 and 72 hours, you rely more and more on fat and ketone bodies for fuel. Your body will prioritize burning fatty acids, sparing ketone bodies to be used by the brain. Any remaining liver glycogen will be fully used up. Glucose production from gluconeogenesis peaks shortly after the 24 hour mark at around 150 g/d and then drops off, settling around 80 g/d after 48 hours. As ketosis gets underway, levels of ketone bodies in your blood will begin to elevate indicating you're in a state of “fasting ketosis.”

Deep Dive

“Ketone bodies” refer to three distinct types of molecules: acetone, acetoacetate, and beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB). Your body can use both acetoacetate and BHB for energy production. Blood ketone meters, which you may have seen people use while fasting or on a ketogenic diet, measure BHB levels in the blood. Throughout the third day of a fast, ghrelin levels (particularly the form of ghrelin thought to be influential for appetite) tend to be consistently low, about as low as after a meal.

It's important to note here the difference between ketosis (a natural metabolic pathway that allows you to burn ketones for fuel) and ketoacidosis (dangerously acidic blood from overly high ketone levels, usually only seen in alcoholics, diabetics, and cases of extreme starvation). Ketoacidosis normally occurs only when both blood glucose and blood ketone levels are elevated, which is unlikely to occur during a fast. BHB levels will vary based on the individual, but within 24-72 hours of fasting, you're likely to see BHB levels rise to somewhere between 0.5 - 2.0 mM, safely in the “fasting ketosis” range.

With diminishing nutrients and growth factors like protein, glucose and insulin along with increased AMPK activity, autophagy is able to ramp up its process of cellular cleanup.

Deep Ketosis

72+ hours

1 Liver continues to produce ketones which become the primary fuel for the brain.

2 IGF-1 levels are low, as is glucose and circulating insulin.

3 BHB production increases.

4 Total ghrelin output remains lower than baseline.

5 Autophagy accelerates.

You are now in the Prolonged Fasting Zone. After day 3, glucose and insulin levels remain low, hunger stays suppressed, and you're in a steady state of physiological ketosis. You'll also see a decrease in something called IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor). IGF-1 is a hormone involved in growth and development. When nutrients are restricted, your liver decreases its IGF-1 production.

Deep Dive

Fasting for 3+ days has been shown to decrease circulating insulin and glucose by 30% or more, which can contribute to decreased risk of metabolic disease. Blood BHB will continue to elevate. One study showed that BHB levels will eventually plateau between 5-6 mM by day 20-25 of fasting. (NOTE: If you're contemplating an extremely long fast, it’s worth consulting with a medical professional to make sure you're doing so safely.) As your BHB levels rise, your hunger should diminish. A 2020 review of the scientific literature showed a correlation between elevated BHB levels and lower levels of hunger, although the underlying mechanisms are currently unknown.

Inhibiting IGF-1 is another step toward taking the brakes off your cellular cleanup process. This deep into your fast, all the nutrients and growth signals that inhibit autophagy continue to diminish until they reach a plateau.
