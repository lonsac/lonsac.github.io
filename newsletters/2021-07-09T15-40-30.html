Greetings from Oaxaca!

I'm flying back to the States today after a month in Mexico. Between the warm people we met, the delicious food we ate, and the copious amounts of mezcal we consumed, it was a fantastic trip.

Here’s what I want to share this week:

​How Philosophers Think: One of the peculiar things about writing essays is that you never know what's going to stick with you. Intellectual Grace, a term I coined while writing this piece, has become a cardinal virtue of mine. Beyond the term, this essay is a comprehensive outline of how studying philosophy shapes your thinking.

​How Jeff Bezos Solved the Succession Problem: With Bezos passing the CEO crown to his successor, Andy Jassy, I made a video about how he thinks about transitioning power. Throughout history, such as in ancient Rome and modern-day Japan, CEOs adopt children who assume the responsibilities of leadership. But adopting kids for the sake of business doesn't fly in America.

So, how did Bezos create a company that could thrive without his day-to-day involvement?

Today's Finds

​Krista Tippett: Some people make the world a better place just by being exactly who they are. Krista Tippett is one such person. Her podcast, On Being, is like a touchstone to me. It pairs the presence of meditation with the awakening of scripture. My all-time favorite interview is the one with John O'Donahue. It inspired my essay: The Fruits of Friendship.

​Ten Ways to Destroy the Imagination of Your Child: I found this book through an excellent Twitter thread. I looked up the author, saw that he was famous for translating Dante, and immediately purchased the book. It's about the way modernity suppresses childhood imagination. My favorites are "cut all heroes down to size" and "replace the fairy tale with political cliches and fads." Awe and wonder are under-rated virtues.

José Ricón: I met José at an Emergent Ventures meetup a couple of years ago. It took me ~30 seconds to realize he had one of the sharpest minds I've ever come across. He can process information faster than just about anybody I've ever met. His posts are long, but if you find one that interests you, I can almost guarantee that it's one of the best introductions to the subject online. If you're interested in education, I recommend this primer on Bloom's Two-Sigma Problem. And if you're interested in cities, I recommend his post on rising construction costs.

​J. Cole: I took a Hip-Hop appreciation class during my freshman year of high school. I still remember one of the seniors doing a non-stop freestyle for upwards of 90 minutes. It was amazing. How did he come up with ideas so fast? This video of J. Cole gave me the same sensation. He exudes a special magic in the room — the kind you only find when you combine natural talent and cultivated excellence.

Roger Scruton, On Beauty: When Roger Scruton passed away, Nassim Taleb wrote: “Fiercely independent, [Roger Scruton] was one of the last thinkers who used their own head.” I didn’t know who he was, so I watched as many of his interviews on YouTube as I could find. I also read his book Introduction to Beauty: A Very Short Introduction and watched his documentary on the same subject — both of which are excellent. Start with the documentary, though. It's superb.
