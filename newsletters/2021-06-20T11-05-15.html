Survivors of China’s Mao and North Korea Speak Out on Woke Schools

What happened:

Two unrelated women who each survived brutal authoritarian regimes have recently spoken out against woke schools, and critical race theory.

Last month Simon wrote a Notes from the Field article comparing Critical Race Theory being taught in schools to the Cultural Revolution under Mao’s China starting in 1966.

In China, children were told to denounce teachers and peers for harboring bourgeois sympathies— like wearing nice clothes, or owning a radio.

Xi Van Fleet lived through it, and said she witnessed it pit students against teachers and each other. They were encouraged to inform on anyone not properly devoted to Mao or Communism.

She witnessed teachers spit on and attacked violently, as well as parents sent to jail after their own children denounced them.

Van Fleet told the Loudoun County school board in Virginia that "The Communist regime used the same critical theory to divide people. The only difference is they used class instead of race."

"This is indeed the American version of the Chinese Cultural Revolution. The critical race theory has its roots in cultural Marxism. It should have no place in our school."

We talked about Loudoun County in April after parents and teachers created an enemies list of parents who criticized — or simply did not praise — Critical Race Theory.

Unfortunately, the woke indoctrination continues into college — as Simon has also pointed out.

Yeonmi Park escaped from North Korea when she was 13 years old. When she finally made it to the US to attend college at Columbia University, she loved nothing more than reading history, and classic literature — which was banned in North Korea.

When a professor asked who read Jane Austin, Park raised her hand, thinking it was a good thing. But she was told that classic literature was subconsciously brainwashing her into a colonial, racist mindset.

Park says, “I literally crossed the Gobi Desert to be free and I realized I’m not free, America’s not free... they are forcing you to think the way they want you to think. I realized, wow, this is insane. I thought America was different but I saw so many similarities to what I saw in North Korea that I started worrying.”

She says that in North Korea, people don’t have access to information. In the US, it is available, but people choose to be brainwashed. “Education almost makes you not able to think critically.”

“Because I have seen oppression I know what it looks like.”

Park says, “Where are we going from here? The future of our country is as bleak as North Korea if we do not rise up right now.”

What this means:

These are harrowing warnings to hear from people who have lived through some of the worst oppressive regimes in recent history.

Luckily, we are not in the same position of North Koreans, or Chinese under Mao. We can move ourselves and our families out of town, out of state, and out of the country.

And it is not 1900— we are not dependent on the government or universities to obtain an education.

But What About College?

How do we prepare ourselves, and our children, to be successful and productive in the world, if they are being indoctrinated with parasitic ideologies?

Sure to become a doctor or a lawyer, you still need a degree.

But it makes less and less sense to spend hundreds of thousands of dollars to, at best, receive a piece of paper, and at worst, have your mind turned to mush and your future prospects robbed.

There are still some holdouts — Hillsdale College is a classic example of an institution which still attempts to teach and not indoctrinate. It doesn’t even accept government subsidies.

But in many cases, college might just not be the best option anymore.

Imagine spending that money instead on starting a business. Even if it fails, that is likely time better spent than majoring in underwater basket weaving.

Or start smaller. To learn how to hire and manage employees, you can spend $100 on websites like Fiverr, Freelancer, or Upwork to hire contractors for one time jobs — video editors, website designers, copywriters, etc.

The reverse is also true: most people don’t have to earn a boatload of money just out of high school. Teens could pick a skill they already have or are interested in, and start freelancing.

Of course it helps to have a mentor. But that is also something young adults can find on their own. Figuring out how to effectively network and prove you can provide value is part of the learning process.

Finding a job right out of high school even remotely related to what you might want to do in the future gives you a four year head start on building job skills.

Coders and IT specialists have known for years that they don’t need to go to college. They are often self-taught or enroll in specific classes.

And if an 18 year old isn’t sure what they want to do with their life — imagine that — there are countless programs that will have them traveling, meeting new people, and learning something new.

For example, a website called Work Away connects travelers willing to work with hosts around the world looking for help on farms, in schools, and on other projects.

The main point is that individuals are more empowered than ever before to take their education into their own hands.

And by doing so, they are more likely to come away with valuable skills, and a true education.
