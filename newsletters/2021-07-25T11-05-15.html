3 Reasons a Global Food Crisis Looms, and the Most Resilient Countries

What happened:

There are at least three pressures on food supplies around the world right now.

1. Food shortages because of riots and looting:

Last week, Sovereign Man team member Andre Bothma shared his first-hand experience of the riots and looting which broke out across South Africa.

Rioters have destroyed shops, trucks, and even entire fields of crops. Looters have stolen about $340 million worth of food and other goods. Riots have shut down highways, so farmers cannot bring fresh produce and meat to market.

Over a week later, many grocery store shelves are still empty, while others have to ration food to customers who wait in line for hours.

Tens of thousands of businesses have been affected and hundreds of thousands of jobs lost because of the destruction. No one knows how long it will take the supply chain to recover.

2. COVID + Communism = Food shortages:

Okay, communism alone is pretty good at creating food shortages. But add some extra authoritarianism in the name of COVID-19, and it makes the situation even worse.

Cuba is facing its worst food crisis in 25 years. Cuba imports 70% of its food, but prices have risen sharply over the past year. And without tourism, Cuba does not have the money to import enough food.

Meanwhile the government’s currency controls and trade restrictions hamper any effort Cubans could make to organize a market for supplies on their own.

For example, the government forces farmers to sell goods to the government below market value, and imposes idiotic livestock management rules. Cubans have to apply for permission from bureaucrats to slaughter their own cows.

3. COVID restrictions break the supply chain:

The US and other Western countries are facing issues with the food supply chain because of COVID lockdowns, restrictions, and worker shortages.

Last week, a UK government contact tracing app told hundreds of thousands of workers to self-isolate because they may have come into contact with someone who had COVID-19.

As a result, supply chains have been affected, some grocery store shelves are empty, and other supermarkets have closed altogether. BP (British Petroleum) had to shut down some stations due to a shortage of drivers to deliver fuel.

In the US, some industries are having trouble hiring workers because of lavish unemployment benefits, eviction moratoriums, and other COVID cash handouts from the government.

This has led to Taco Bell, for example, telling customers they are experiencing shortages and some items may be unavailable. At some locations this has included beef, chicken, and fresh vegetables... which leaves little left to make a taco.

What this means:

All these factors compound.

COVID restrictions, riots, and destruction lead to job losses and economic downturns. Tough economic times lead to civil unrest... which can lead to restrictions on civil liberties, further inflaming the public and disrupting supply chains.

COVID restrictions, even rules like requiring a doorman to spray you down with alcohol, or limiting restaurant capacity, require more workers, more red tape, and more time and money wasted on compliance.

When the government can arbitrarily close restaurants at any random time, this also affects how food items are consumed, which leads to waste during the shutdown, and shortages when everything suddenly reopens.

Even if we somehow move beyond this ugly COVID chapter, a lot of damage is already done.

Unfortunately, the civil unrest this all causes becomes a contagion in itself, and is likely to only increase before subsiding.

What you can do about it:

These situations have people thinking about the basics — where does my food actually come from?

When deliberately choosing where you want to live, it makes sense to consider how resilient your country is against food shortages.

There are different ways to measure this.

For example, a 2013 study found that the US, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and most of South America and eastern Europe have the arable land and water required to grow all of the food they each currently consume. (That doesn’t mean they do produce all of what they consume... more below.)

In contrast, places like Mexico, UK, Japan, Italy, and Portugal do not have enough land to produce all the food the population consumes— they rely on the international supply chain.

But to see which countries currently produce enough food for themselves, we can look at net food imports and exports.

Net imports suggest a country does not currently grow enough food to feed its population, while net exports suggest there is enough produced internally to feed the country.

One map, created by Zeihan on Geopolitics consulting firm using USDA and UN data, looks at imports and exports versus consumption of staple crops like “raw maize, wheat, rice, soybeans, and other minor grains.” The metric is based on calories (as opposed to cost or weight).

Food Secure: The US, Canada, Australia, Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, and Russia export 35% or more of these crops they produce.

France and most of eastern Europe export 10-34.9% of their production, while India, Germany, and South Africa break even, consuming the same amount they produce.

Food Insecure: The UK, Spain, China, Turkey, and Tanzania import 10-24.9% of the calories consumed from these crops.

Mexico, Italy, Ecuador, and Norway import between 25-49.9%, and Chile, Panama, Portugal, New Zealand, and Japan import 50-74.9% of these staples.

BUT the picture changes in another map that considers ALL agricultural imports and exports, using data from the CIA World Factbook and World Bank. The metric is based on cost— expenditures on imports, versus income on exports.

Food Secure Net Exporters: Argentina, Brazil, Ecuador, Uruguay, and New Zealand are some of the biggest next exporters of food, followed by Chile, Peru, Australia,Turkey, Ukraine, and Tanzania.

Food Secure Break Even: The US, Canada, Mexico, Panama, France, Spain, and Germany all roughly break even on food import expenditures, versus income from exports.

Food Insecure Net Importers: Japan, the UK, Portugal, and Sweden are all net importers of food.

Keep in mind that this data has limitations.

For example it does not address the variety of imports and exports, such as meat and dairy protein, versus fruit and vegetable nutrients.

Also, net imports/exports measured by price does not tell the whole story, since high price low calorie foods, or vice versa, could skew the calorie balance.

Still, it provides a helpful starting point to understand various countries’ food security.

But if you’re the homesteading type that wants to live off the land, another question is: in what countries could you produce all the food you need — or at least enough to not entirely rely on functioning markets and supply chains?

In that case, we need to look not just at the fertility of land, but also land and water use rights.
