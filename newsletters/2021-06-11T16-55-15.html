​Idea Rivers: Writing is harder than it looks because readers assume that ideas take a clear and obvious path, while writers know all the dead ends they traversed to reach their final destination. The simpler an idea looks, the crazier the journey probably was. Here's my short Twitter thread on the subject, which I plan to elaborate on soon.

Gresham's Law: Online content suffers from Gresham’s Law. Using a classic Econ 101 argument, you’d think that competition would guarantee an improvement in the quality of content. But in practice, the opposite has happened (which I talked about on Twitter yesterday). This is one of the central ideas in my essay, News in the Age of Abundance.

Mind Control: Tiger Woods' biography is one of the best books I've read in the past few years. Inspired by it, I just published a new YouTube video about how Tiger Woods uses mind control. Today's Finds

​The Tyranny of Time: There are two kinds of time: body time and mechanical time. Body time moves by the rhythm of your heart. You eat when you're hungry and sleep when you're tired. It moves fast when we're in the flow and slowly when we're bored. Mechanical time is the opposite. It always moves at the same speed and does not respond to the needs of individuals. When I'm relaxing, I try to be on body time as much as possible. That's why I don't wear a watch and why there are no clocks in my apartment. Though people used to protest against time, we've accepted it as an inevitable part of the modern world. This essay shows how that happened.

​Manufacturing Consent: My favorite Noam Chomsky bit comes from the year 1996. In it, he speaks with a BBC reporter and offers the best summary of the way the media manufactures consent. Chomsky says that people learn to censor themselves in school. The people who rise to the top of institutions are the ones who speak in accordance with what they're taught to think. At the end of the clip, the interviewer asks: “How do you know I’m self-censoring?” To which, Chomsky replies: "I’m sure you believe everything you’re saying. But what I’m saying is that if you believed something different, you wouldn’t be sitting where you’re sitting." If this topic is of interest, you'll like my News in the Age of Abundance essay.

​Losing Your Freedom: This one is heavy. When my friend texted it to me, he said: "I need a cigarette with this." It's written by Ross Ulbricht, who founded the Silk Road marketplace. He wrote this speech from prison because he's been given a lifetime sentence without parole. It's worth reading, no matter what you think about him. He talks about the way we dehumanize prisoners and the torture of losing your freedom. If you prefer audio, listen to this.

​McLuhan Unbound: This is a hidden gem, but I'm not sure how to describe it. I could call it a book, but it's too short for that. I could call it a collection of pamphlets, but the ideas are too sophisticated for that too. We'll go with an anthology of essays. They were written in the most creative period of Marshall McLuhan's life after he published The Gutenberg Galaxy but before he published his seminal book: Understanding Media. I like it both because it's colorful (which means I can keep it in my living room) and because it's designed to be read for 10 minutes at a time. Since it's so easy to read, it doubles as spaced repetition for one of my favorite writers. If you're looking for an intro to McLuhan, I also recommend The Medium is the Massage and this talk with Terrance McKenna.

​Michael Sugrue: I recommended this in Friday Finds two weeks ago and I feel called to recommend it again. His YouTube channel is the best thing I've found on the Internet in months. If you're interested in the canon of Western ideas, give it a shot. You won't regret it. If you don't know where to start, I recommend this video about Nietzsche's Critique of Christianity.
