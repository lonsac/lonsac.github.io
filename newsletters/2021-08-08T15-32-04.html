What is Fat Adaptation

For many people, like myself, that grew up striving to emphasize carbohydrates in the diet and eat every couple hours, it is remarkable that the body is capable of switching its priority fuel source so efficiently. Fasters frequently see or hear the term(s) “fat- adapted” or “flip the metabolic switch”, but what do these phrases mean? Why might it benefit you to become more fat-adapted?

Fat-Adaptation: state whereby your body selectively burns fat rather than carbohydrates as the priority fuel source

Metabolic Switch: point in the fast when glycogen balance is reduced and fueling shifts toward emphasis on fatty acids (usually 12+ hours into fast)

Metabolic Flexibility: distinct ability to utilize different fuels (ie. carbohydrate or fat) and to transition smoothly between them

Ketosis: metabolic state where ketone body concentrations are elevated in the blood to aid in providing fuel to organs like the brain

Keto-Adaptation: process in which the body becomes accustomed to utilizing ketones as a preferred fuel. This process can take weeks or months depending upon general health or performance goals

What is Fat-Adaptation?

Fat-adaptation occurs when you consistently engage in endurance training or you consistently limit carbohydrates in the diet (while increasing fat consumption) or both!

Fat adaptation is simply your body’s way of altering metabolism to prioritize fat use due to exercise demands or in response to having reduced carbohydrate energy. The most abundant stored fuel source your body has is fat. Therefore, if you have fasted for 12 or more hours, you are likely to begin mobilizing fat for fuel.

Unfortunately, the Standard American Diet (SAD) generally suppresses fat-adaptation, primarily due to the abundance of refined carbohydrate and sugar as well as constant snacking. While the SAD promotes fat storage, fat-adaptation helps improve your body's capacity to use stored fat for fuel.

Why Become Fat-Adapted?

If you become fat adapted, you are less likely to store fat on the body since you can mobilize and burn fat more easily. Additionally, fat aptation is associated with better metabolic flexibility, which essentially describes the ability to switch between fuel sources with ease based on what's available. Metabolic flexibility is precisely what your body needs when stressed (illness, injury, etc.) to keep you fit and healthy. Think of metabolic flexibility as your body’s energy back-up plan.

How to Become Fat Adapted?

There are several ways you may become more fat adapted. You can do so by fasting, reducing carbohydrate intake, exercise training, or a combination of these.

Fasting - Abstaining from Calorie intake will gradually shift metabolism towards increased reliance on fat, as glycogen stores are depleted. Generally, fat metabolism becomes the primary fuel at the conclusion of your Catabolic fasting zone (around 16 hours). However, there are several factors that might influence this timing, including pre-fast nutrition, body size, and physical activity.

Carbohydrate Restriction - When carbohydrate intake is reduced, it is usually accompanied by an increase in dietary fat and/or protein consumption. Your body intuitively responds to a low-carbohydrate, high-fat (LCHF) diet by upregulating fat metabolism while at rest and during all low-to-moderate intensity exercise.

Each meal impacts which fuel gets priority. Not surprisingly, a single high carbohydrate meal will increase carbohydrate preference at rest. The same is true for exercise. In other words, eating carbs = burning carbs.

Training Status - trained individuals have higher fat oxidation rates than untrained, and will store more fats in the muscle tissue for ease of access and direct use during activity. Additionally, trained individuals have increased mitochondria (number and quality) and enzymes that work to create energy from fat.

There’s no consensus, currently, on a distinct measure that tells you whether you're fat adapted. This is in-part due to overlapping metabolic changes that take place. It’s also because fat adaptation is a process. Although you can increase fat metabolism transiently, like flipping a “metabolic switch”, fat- adaptation takes more time to develop.

There are, however, a few metrics you can track to gain some insight about your progress toward fat-adaptation. They can be broken into two different categories: “objective measures,” which are more quantifiable and “subjective signs,” which are more open-ended.

Objective Measures:

  • Blood Glucose - Although it sounds counter-intuitive, Glucose might be the easiest and most economical test to evaluate fat-adaptation. Reduction in reliance on glucose with fat-adaptation means a consistent reduction in fasted blood glucose. Previous studies have shown after low carbohydrate consumption for 4-5 weeks fasted blood glucose is reduced in diabteic patients (-36%) and healthy participants (-15%)

  • Ketones - Increased fat metabolism (from exercise, fasting, or reduction in dietary carbohydrate) results in the production of ketone bodies. Your ketone levels slightly increase during an overnight fast, but significant increases are associated with extended fasts and/or low-carbohydrate/ ketogenic diets. Ketone bodies can be measured in the urine, blood, and breath. Blood ketone levels that suggest fat- adaptation is present (or in-process) would be 0.5-3 mM. Breath acetone levels that indicate the same are 5-30 ACEs

  • Resting Respiratory Quotient (RQ) - when we have carbohydrates on board, we burn more glucose and resting RQ is higher (2 0.85), when blood sugar decreases, while fasting or consuming a low carbohydrate diet, resting RQ should be lower (s 0.85).

  • Enzymes - specific enzymes such as Hormone Sensitive Lipase (HSL), HMG CoA Synthase, and Acetyl Carnitine are important for production and transport of lipid-based fuels (fatty acids and ketones). They are increased when blood glucose and insulin levels are low during fasting or in response to low-carbohvdrate diets.

Subjective Signs:

  • Fasting Apprehension - while fasting is not effortless, there are many more difficult tasks people complete all the time. If you perceive fasting as being extremely difficult, it may be an indication of reduced metabolic flexibility meaning that your body cannot as nimbly switch between glucose and fat fueling preference.

  • Fed-state Persistent Hunger - symptoms like hunger or headaches between meals may be a sign that your insulin levels are high and your ability to burn fat is reduced. If you notice these consistently 3-4 hours after eating it could be your body's way of reminding you to consume more food and carbohydrates, because you have increased demand for glucose in order to have continuous energy.

  • Post-Meal Fatigue (aka 2:30 slump) - This is not the rare occurrence where you might over-eat on a holiday and then feel lethargic or desire an afternoon nap. If you experience routine fatigue or lethargy after meal time this might also be evidence that your capacity to use fat as a fuel is fairly low, as you slump after you've burned through your available glucose.

If you don't experience any of the above subjective indicators, you are probably enjoying some fat-adaptation. If you consistently cruise through your fasts, have stable energy levels, can’t remember the last time you had a headache, or rarely experience ravenous hunger you might be fat-adapted!

Does Fat-Adaptation = Ketosis?

The short answer is no. You can become more fat-adapted and not be in ketosis. Trained individuals develop adaptations for increased fat utilization through consistent exercise without changing diet or having increases in BHB concentration. But, as mentioned previously, dietary practices including fasting and ketogenic eating can impact fat- adaptation.

If TRF is a consistent nutrition habit of yours, then you are likely signalling a shift toward fat metabolism and ketone production once your fasts extend past 12 hours. However, during your eating window, if you are not specifically formulating your diet for ketosis, then you are probably not remaining in a state of ketosis for very long. Which is completely fine! Prolonged fasting (>72 hours) can cause a more stable increase in ketone oxidation contributing 30-40% of total energy requirements. Even when in ketosis, fatty acids remain the predominant fuel to working skeletal muscles, and this is contributing to fat-adaptation. So, while fat-adaptation does not require ketosis, ketosis can serve as a marker of positive fat- adaptation progress.

How Long Does Fat-Adaptation Take?

Fat-adaptation is a process that takes some time (usually on the order of 2+ weeks) where key transporters and enzyme concentrations change for improved fat burning capacity.

So, while we can rev up fat metabolism with a single high-fat low-carb meal or a solo fast, one or two of these things will not produce long-term fat-adaption (although it is a good start!). Similar to how you wouldn't expect your muscles to appreciably grow after a single bout of weight lifting, fat-adaptation requires regular metabolic signaling to boost fat- burning whether that be daily fasting, exercise, and/or carbohydrate restriction. Fat- adaptation is the product of lifestyle habits rather than a couple of transient signals.

You can also reduce or lose your fat- adaptation if you suppress fat burning signals primarily through spiking insulin levels in your body. It is reasonable to assume that regular intermittent fasting can be a practical strategy for signaling and maintaining some level of fat- adaptation over time.

Fat-Adaptation helps to support metabolic flexibility, which is a key component to healthy aging. Becoming fat-adapted is a process that takes some time (probably weeks or months). You can signal changes through daily inputs associated with your fast duration, meal composition and exercise habits. Your daily habits play an important role in establishing your metabolic foundation. Investing in fat- adaptation, as an energy “back-up plan,” can help you to meet challenges or stress more effectively down the road.
