March to the beat of your body's natural rhythms
Circadian rhythms, your body’s 24-hour cycles of biological, hormonal, and behavioral patterns, drive a slew of physiological processes, including the production of hormones that regulate sleep, hunger, and metabolism. Ultimately, they influence your body weight, performance, and susceptibility to disease.
 
When and how much you sleep, eat, or exercise (and how these activities affect your health) are intrinsically linked to these rhythms.
 
A key driver of circadian rhythms is melatonin. Often called the sleepiness hormone, melatonin is a protein produced primarily in the pineal gland of the brain. Its levels oscillate throughout the day, typically peaking one hour into sleep and ebbing two hours after waking.
 
Let the sunshine in
The primary signal that flips the switch on melatonin release is light. Receptors in the eye respond to light (or its absence), directing the body to suppress (or release) melatonin.
 
These receptors are particularly sensitive to blue light, a wavelength of light emitted from both natural and artificial sources. In the past, humans' primary exposure to blue light was from the sun, but in modern times, we get it from those ever-present electronic devices – TVs, computer screens, and cell phones – often during times of the day or night that are out of sync with our bodies' natural rhythms.
 
Dimming lights and cutting back on the use of these devices in the evening allows melatonin to rise naturally, so we can sleep.
 
Melatonin has a wide range of influence
It turns out that melatonin influences more than sleep, however. A growing body of evidence suggests that melatonin inhibits insulin secretion, influencing blood glucose levels. As a result, eating too close to bedtime or right after waking – before nightly melatonin levels have returned to daytime levels – may impair your body's ability to process glucose.
 
The relatively narrow window of insulin secretion is an indication that adopting a narrow window of eating might optimize metabolic health – so the body can take a break from digesting and focus on repair and rejuvenation.
 
 
Enter time-restricted eating
Dr. Panda and his colleagues observed that when mice ate within a brief, defined time window ("time-restricted"), they had fewer metabolic diseases, a result of maintaining healthy circadian rhythms. Time-restricted eating shows promise as a strategy to improve health in humans, too.
 
But do you really know how much time you spend eating each day? Dr. Panda's research demonstrates that most people don't, typically under guessing by a few hours. His ongoing research is helping people understand their circadian rhythms and how they affect their health.
 
Paying attention to circadian rhythms can optimize performance
The mere fact that the body has these rigid timetables and schedules suggests that there are times during the day when human performance is optimized. Evidence bears this out in terms of cognitive performance (best in the morning) and exercise benefits (greatest in the afternoon – but modifiable with caffeine).
 
There's so much to say about circadian rhythms, but the bottom line is that working with our circadian rhythms, rather than against them – can have profound impacts on our health. Dr. Panda and I cover a lot of ground in this discussion. You won't want to miss it!
