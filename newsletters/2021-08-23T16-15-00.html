Coolest Things I Learned This Week

Look at Your Fish

One of America’s best biographers keeps a sign over his desk that says: “Look at Your Fish.”

His name is David McCullough and the saying helped him win a Pulitzer Prize. The story goes back to a 19th century Harvard naturalist named Louis Agassiz. He was a teacher. Legend has it that on the first day of class, he’d take a fish out of a smelly jar and drop it on the table.

Then, he’d say: “Look at your fish” and leave the room.

The students would say: "What is he talking about? It’s just a fish.”

After 20 minutes, he would come back and ask the students what they saw.

“Not much,” they’d say. It’s just a fish. It’s literally just a fish.

“Look again.”

Students were encouraged to draw and write about the fish. After a few days, students started to get it. Looking closer, they saw details they’d glossed over their entire lives, from the color of the scales to the texture of the fins. By the end of the semester, they saw how a simple fish was wondrously complex.

McCullough keeps a reminder of that story above his desk and uses it whenever he teaches writing because writers are really professional observationalists. They live in search of ideas that others miss because they don’t look closely enough. Insight comes less from doing crazy things that nobody’s ever done before and more from looking at what’s been on the table the entire time — right in front of everybody.

Look at your fish.

Reading Books

My friend Justin Murphy made a good point recently: “People overestimate how many books other people read.”

The trend may even be accelerating. I’m in a friend group of intellectually curious people, and just about everybody says they’re reading fewer books than they used to. But at the risk of sounding like your 5th grade English teacher, reading books is the easiest way to find good ideas to write about.

A huge percentage of my own writing is repackaging the ideas of 20th-century technology theorists like Walter Ong, Marshall McLuhan, and Lewis Mumford.

My friend Jeremy Giffon, who I co-wrote Saving the Liberal Arts with, says that whenever you’re intimidated by a thinker, you should know that 90% of their ideas are regurgitated from books. Their original thought is the other ten percent. Even better, he says: "One advantage of reading books sequentially is seeing just how rare it is for anyone to actually make a huge leap.”

Taken all together, here’s what this means:
​

  1. Today, the simple act of reading books gives you a huge advantage. If you’re looking for ideas to write about, read more.
    ​
  2. If you can, read old books.
    ​
  3. Reading books sequentially shows you how rare leaps in knowledge really are. Once you see how ideas are remixed and recombined, you’ll feel comfortable building upon the ideas of others — which is what great writers always do.

​Follow the Left Tackle (Fred Wilson)​

Fred Wilson is the founder of Union Square Ventures and one of the most successful venture capitalists of the 21st century so far.

He thinks of investing like a game of football. You have to imagine you’re the running back following the left tackle and looking for a hole that you can run through.

Practically, here’s what this means. Just as running backs look specifically at certain offensive linemen, you should look specifically at certain technologies if you want to see where the world is moving.

I agree with his assessment that the world of crypto is the left tackle to follow. He says: "I am not saying NFTs are the next big thing. I am saying that consumer experiences built on a crypto stack are the next big thing. I am saying that NFT experiences are showing the way. They are the left tackle that you can run behind into the opening. Where enormous opportunity exists."

No matter what kind of work you do, there are a few key players to track. Watch them, wait for the opening to emerge, and run through it when it arrives. Like a running back, you don’t need to create the hole yourself. You just need to follow the left tackle and run through the hole when it opens up.

In Praise of High Standards

When I was in college at Elon, there was never trash on the ground. Nobody littered. People even picked up other people’s trash. In fact, people were so respectful of the campus that they reported loose bricks on the sidewalk to the campus maintenance team.

In retrospect, staying clean wasn’t an explicit guideline. “No litter” signs didn’t exist. You kept the campus clear because everybody else did. Simple as that.

My alma mater understood that social signals are more effective than explicit rules. The maintenance team set a standard that everybody else followed.

Tactically, we’ve brought this lesson to Write of Passage. We want high standards for excellence and feedback among students. This cohort, we have a team of 14 alumni mentors who set these cultural standards. They do so by modeling the best ways to provide feedback, write from conversation, and behave during the live sessions. By shining the spotlight on them, we can create a culture of rigor without explicit rules.

​Clean the Bathrooms​

Speaking of cleanliness, a McDonald's employee was once asked why they had an edge in the fast-food industry.

The employee said: "We prioritize the little things like keeping our bathrooms clean."

"But everybody can do that."

"Yes, but only McDonald's does."

The story may be apocryphal, but the lesson applies everywhere. Sometimes, you just have to nail the basics.

Photo of the Week

​

This photo was taken many years ago, right after I received my college diploma.

My hand was in the air, in part, because I was happy to be done with the traditional education system. I spent the majority of my childhood frustrated with the state of education. My classes bored me, and it wasn't until after college that I developed my passionate love for learning.

In retrospect, my teachers should have taken inspiration more seriously, which is exactly what I try to do in Write of Passage. Inspiration is the keystone of learning. It’s the engine behind a student’s motivation and the glue that makes ideas stick. But because our school system undervalues the necessity of it, students don’t learn as much as they could.

The process of learning is like falling in love with a song. Initially, you’re only attracted to songs that move you emotionally. If they’re catchy, you’ll listen to them enough to get stuck in your head. If the song keeps resonating with you, you’ll learn about the artist and explore the lyrics in depth. Talk to an obsessive and in addition to singing the lyrics for you, they’ll tell you the backstory behind the music.
